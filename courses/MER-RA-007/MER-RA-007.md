

# Heating System Assessment for Retrofit: What the Retrofit Assessor Must Record
**Course ID:** MER-RA-007 | **CPD Hours:** 1.0 | **Published:** March 2026
*© Meridian CPD 2026. All rights reserved.*

---

## Learning Objectives

- Record all mandatory heating system data fields required by PAS 2035:2023 Annex A, including system type, fuel, age, efficiency, controls, and heat emitter characteristics.
- Assess heat emitter systems (radiators, underfloor heating, fan convectors) in sufficient detail to inform heat pump readiness assessment at the design stage.
- Identify the key heating system data gaps that block heat pump design and record data in a format that supports downstream design decision-making.
- Recognise the indicators of overheating risk and heating system adequacy for the current and post-retrofit dwelling loads.
- Distinguish the Retrofit Assessor's data recording role from the Retrofit Designer's system selection and specification role.

---

## Introduction

Heating system data is arguably the most design-critical information the Retrofit Assessor collects. Fabric measures improve the thermal performance of the building envelope; heating system replacement determines what replaces the fuel source. In an era of heat pump roll-out, electrification of heat, and the managed decline of gas, the heating data in the Retrofit Assessment is the foundation for decisions that will cost the occupant — or the programme funder — between £5,000 and £20,000 or more.

Inadequate heating system data in Retrofit Assessments is one of the most consistently cited design-stage problems across funded retrofit programmes. Heat pump designers, in particular, require specific data about heat emitters, flow temperatures, and system hydraulics that assessors without a heating background frequently do not collect. The result is that designers are forced to make conservative assumptions — often specifying larger heat pumps than necessary, or requiring a full emitter replacement that a better assessment would have shown was unnecessary.

This course covers what PAS 2035:2023 requires the assessor to record about heating systems, what heat pump readiness assessment data looks like in practice, and how to collect it systematically on site.

## Section 1: Primary Heating System — Mandatory Data Fields

**System type.** The primary heating system type must be identified and recorded using the PAS 2035:2023 and RdSAP 10 classification categories: wet central heating (gas/oil/LPG boiler — combi/regular/system), electric storage heaters, direct electric heating, heat pump (air source/ground source), district heat network, warm air system, or solid fuel system. Where the system type is hybrid (e.g., boiler plus heat pump), both elements must be recorded.

**Fuel type.** Mains gas, LPG (including bulk tank vs cylinder supply), heating oil (kerosene), wood pellet/log/chip, electricity (grid), and district heat are the primary categories. For properties with multiple fuel types (gas boiler plus immersion for backup, oil boiler plus log burner as secondary), all fuel types and their role in the system must be recorded.

**Manufacturer, model, and serial number.** The boiler data plate (typically on the front of the boiler under the front cover, or on a sticker on the boiler casing) contains the manufacturer name, model, and serial number. These allow the designer to look up the product in the Product Characteristics Database (PCDB) and obtain accurate efficiency data. Where the data plate is missing or illegible, the approximate installation year and visible characteristics (burner type, flue type, apparent age) should be recorded. Do not estimate the model — record what is visible and note what could not be identified.

**Installation year.** The installation year is used to estimate system efficiency where PCDB data is not available, and to assess the system's remaining operational life (relevant to whether heating system replacement forms part of the retrofit design). Acceptable evidence: data plate, service record, occupant documentation. Where not documented, the occupant's best estimate is recorded with a note that it is an estimate.

**Efficiency.** For gas and oil boilers: the seasonal efficiency (SEDBUK) or ErP rating where available from the PCDB or manufacturer documentation. For storage heaters: the heat retention index and type (standard or high heat retention). For heat pumps: the Seasonal Coefficient of Performance (SCOP) from manufacturer data. Where the product is not identifiable and efficiency data is unavailable, the age-band default is applied and must be noted as such.

**Controls.** The controls specification directly affects the calculated system efficiency in RdSAP 10 and the retrofit design options. Mandatory controls data: room thermostat (presence, type, and location), TRVs (presence and coverage — all radiators, some radiators, none), programmer (time and temperature programming capability), weather compensation (yes/no), load compensation (yes/no). For heat pumps: presence of smart controls or heat pump optimisation software. Controls upgrade is often a low-cost, high-impact retrofit measure in itself, and accurate controls recording ensures it is not overlooked.

## Section 2: Heat Emitter Assessment — The Critical Data for Heat Pump Readiness

The single most common gap in Retrofit Assessments for properties proposed for heat pump installation is inadequate heat emitter data. A heat pump operates at lower flow temperatures than a gas boiler — typically 45–55°C flow vs 70–80°C for a conventional gas boiler. Whether existing radiators can deliver adequate heat output at these lower temperatures depends on their size (surface area) relative to the calculated heat load of the room they serve.

**What must be recorded for each heat emitter:**
- Type: steel panel radiator, cast iron radiator, aluminium radiator, underfloor heating (wet or electric), fan convector, skirting heater, or other
- Physical size: height and width (and number of panels/fins for multi-panel radiators). For underfloor heating: the floor area served
- Location: which room and which wall — this allows the designer to assess emitter distribution relative to room heat loss
- Condition: any visible physical damage, suspected airlocks (cool patches), or apparent non-function
- TRV or lockshield valve type where identifiable

This data allows the designer to calculate (or estimate) the existing radiator output at the design flow temperature and compare it against the room heat loss. Where existing radiators are undersized at 45°C flow, the design must specify replacement or supplementary radiators. Where they are adequate, the heat pump can be installed without emitter replacement — a significant cost saving.

**Underfloor heating.** Properties with underfloor heating (wet, in screed) are typically well-suited for heat pump installation because UFH operates at low flow temperatures (30–40°C) compatible with heat pump output. The assessor must record: coverage (which rooms, zone or not), approximate installation year, manifold location, and any known operational issues. The presence of UFH is a strong positive indicator for heat pump viability.

**Mixed emitter systems.** Many properties have a mix of radiators (upstairs) and UFH (ground floor, typically installed during a kitchen or extension renovation). This mixed system requires careful emitter recording to identify the temperature constraints of each zone, which will drive the heat pump flow temperature selection at design stage.

## Section 3: System Hydraulics, Age, and Replacement Indicators

**Pipe sizing and material.** The existing pipework constrains flow rates and, in older systems, chemical composition. The assessor should record: visible pipe material (copper, MDPE/plastic, old steel), approximate pipe sizing where visible (15 mm, 22 mm, 28 mm supply main), and any visible scale, corrosion, or suspected system contamination (dark or opaque water in the system, visible sludge at drain points). Chemical contamination and scale in the existing system can damage heat pump components — a system flush is typically required as a pre-installation condition.

**System age and remaining life.** The installation year of the boiler is the primary age indicator. A boiler installed more than 15 years ago is approaching end of life. The assessor records the age but does not make a determination about whether the system should be replaced — that is a design decision. The data point (age, condition, known service history) is what the assessment provides.

**Hot water cylinder.** For properties with a separate hot water cylinder (i.e., not combi boilers), the cylinder must be assessed for heat pump compatibility. Key data: cylinder type (direct/indirect), volume (litres), immersion heater position (top, bottom, both), thermostat position and setting, jacket insulation thickness. For heat pump installation, the existing cylinder may need to be replaced with a dedicated heat pump cylinder (which has a larger coil surface area to work with the lower flow temperatures). Recording cylinder specifications allows the designer to determine this.

**Pressurised vs gravity-fed systems.** The distinction between a sealed pressurised system (expansion vessel, pressure gauge, filling loop) and a gravity-fed open-vented system (header tank in the loft) is a design-relevant data point. Sealed systems are generally preferred for heat pump installation; open-vented systems may require conversion. The assessor records which type is present.

## Section 4: Overheating Risk and Heating Adequacy Assessment

**Overheating risk.** PAS 2035:2023 requires the assessor to consider overheating risk — the risk that a retrofitted dwelling becomes uncomfortably hot in summer due to increased insulation and reduced ventilation. The heating system assessment contributes to this: a property with a large, well-insulated and airtight fabric that has solar-gain potential (large south-facing glazing, for example) is at higher risk of summer overheating. The assessor records observable risk factors: orientation and area of south-facing glazing, absence of solar shading, and any history of overheating reported by the occupant. This data flags the issue for the designer to address.

**Heating adequacy.** Is the current heating system adequate for the current occupancy and dwelling configuration? Where the occupant reports rooms that cannot be adequately heated, or where the assessor observes that rooms lack functional heat emitters, this is a data point. It may indicate undersized emitters, poor system circulation, or controls that prevent adequate heat distribution. Recording this informs decisions about whether emitter upgrade or system reconfiguration forms part of the retrofit design.

**Secondary heating.** Electric panel heaters, plug-in oil-filled radiators, or solid fuel stoves as secondary sources are common in properties where the primary system is inadequate. These should be recorded — they indicate a system adequacy problem and, if unaddressed in the retrofit design, may result in high-cost secondary heating continuing post-retrofit.

## Key Takeaways

- All mandatory heating data fields — system type, fuel, manufacturer/model, year, efficiency, and controls — must be recorded from physical evidence, not from occupant recall alone.
- Heat emitter data (type, size by height and width, location, condition) is the most critical data for heat pump readiness assessment and is consistently the biggest gap in Retrofit Assessments.
- UFH is a positive heat pump viability indicator; mixed emitter systems require zone-specific recording to inform flow temperature design.
- Hot water cylinder specifications are mandatory for heat pump projects — cylinder volume, coil size, and immersion configuration determine whether cylinder replacement is needed.
- System age, condition, and pipe material are design-relevant data points — the assessor records them; the designer determines replacement strategy.
- Overheating risk indicators (south-facing glazing, airtightness improvements, absence of shading) must be recorded as part of the heating and fabric assessment.

## Self-Assessment Questions

**Q1.** A Retrofit Assessor surveys a 1985 semi-detached house with a 2014 gas combi boiler and 12 double-panel radiators. The property is proposed for heat pump installation. What additional data must the assessor collect that is frequently omitted?

A) The gas tariff the occupant is currently paying
B) The Seasonal Coefficient of Performance of the existing boiler
C) The boiler service history
D) The height and width of each radiator, their location, and the presence of TRVs — to enable heat pump flow temperature and emitter adequacy assessment

**Correct answer: D**

*Radiator sizing data (height, width, panel type) is critical for heat pump design but is frequently omitted from assessments. Without it, the designer cannot assess whether existing radiators will deliver adequate output at heat pump flow temperatures and must either assume replacement or request a return visit.*

---

**Q2.** A property has a sealed pressurised central heating system with visible black sludge visible at a drain point. What must the assessor record, and what design implication does this have?

A) Advise the client to have the system flushed before the survey
B) Record the system as "unsuitable for heat pump" — contamination is an absolute barrier
C) Record the presence of visible system contamination (sludge at drain point), note the system type as sealed/pressurised, and flag system flushing as a likely pre-installation condition — particularly relevant for heat pump installation where contamination can damage the compressor
D) Record the system as "functional" — sludge does not affect design decisions

**Correct answer: C**

*System contamination (sludge, scale, debris) is a mandatory data observation and a relevant pre-installation condition. The assessor records what is observed and flags the pre-condition. Whether flushing resolves the issue, or whether it disqualifies the system from heat pump installation, is a design determination — not an assessment determination.*

---

**Q3.** A property has a combi boiler installed in 2016. There is no hot water cylinder. The property is proposed for an air source heat pump with hot water provision. What data gap does the assessor need to address?

A) The property will require a hot water cylinder for heat pump DHW provision; the assessor should identify available space and location for a cylinder, and record this as a design-relevant space constraint
B) None — combi boiler properties do not need hot water assessment
C) This is a Retrofit Coordinator decision, not an assessment data requirement
D) The assessor should advise the client that a cylinder will be needed

**Correct answer: A**

*Heat pumps require a hot water cylinder for domestic hot water provision — they do not function as combi-style instant hot water heaters in most configurations. The assessor should note the absence of a cylinder and identify available space for one (airing cupboard size, loft space, utility room). This is design-relevant data that affects the feasibility of heat pump installation.*

---

**Q4.** An assessor surveys a 1970s bungalow with a gas boiler and all-rooms underfloor heating installed in a screed in 2018. What does this tell the designer about heat pump viability?

A) The high thermal mass of screed UFH prevents heat pump operation
B) The property is likely well-suited for heat pump installation, as wet UFH in screed operates at low flow temperatures (30–40°C) compatible with heat pump output; the assessor should record the UFH area coverage, manifold location, and installation year
C) The assessor cannot determine heat pump viability — this is a designer role
D) UFH is incompatible with heat pumps — the assessment should flag this as a barrier

**Correct answer: B**

*Wet underfloor heating in screed operates at low flow temperatures (typically 30–40°C) that are directly compatible with heat pump output, making this property a good candidate. The assessor records the UFH data (coverage, manifold, year, condition); the designer uses it to confirm suitability and specify the system.*

---

**Q5.** The occupant of a 1960s mid-terrace reports that the front bedroom "has never been warm enough." The radiator in the room is a single-panel unit, 600 mm × 400 mm, serving a north-facing room with a double-glazed bay window. What should the assessor record?

A) Record the radiator size (600 × 400 mm, single panel), location (north-facing bedroom), the occupant's report of inadequate heating, and flag the potential emitter under-sizing for the designer to assess against calculated room heat loss
B) The occupant's complaint is subjective — no data recording is required
C) Recommend a larger radiator in the assessment report
D) Record the room as "adequate" — single-panel radiators are standard in bedrooms

**Correct answer: A**

*The occupant report of inadequate heating is a data point that must be recorded, as is the specific radiator size and location. The designer will calculate whether the radiator is undersized for the room heat loss, particularly at lower heat pump flow temperatures. The assessor records; the designer determines adequacy.*
