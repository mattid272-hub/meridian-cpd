

# Non-Domestic MEES 2031: EPC B Target and Implications for Assessors

## Learning Objectives

- Identify the revised MEES compliance thresholds and key milestone dates for non-domestic private rented sector properties through to 2031
- Explain which non-domestic buildings fall within scope of the 2031 EPC B requirement and which exemptions may apply
- Assess the practical impact of the EPC B target on non-domestic EPC production methodology and recommendation reports
- Evaluate common upgrade pathways for non-domestic buildings currently rated C to E and advise landlords on realistic improvement strategies
- Recognise the expanded role and professional responsibilities of energy assessors in supporting landlord compliance with tightened MEES regulations

## Introduction

The Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards framework for non-domestic private rented sector buildings is undergoing its most significant escalation since the original 2018 regulations prohibited the letting of properties rated F or G. The UK Government's confirmation that larger non-domestic buildings must achieve an EPC rating of B by 2031 fundamentally changes the compliance landscape — not incrementally, but structurally.

Under the existing regime, a landlord with a D-rated office block faces no MEES barrier to letting. Under the 2031 framework, that same building becomes non-compliant. The gap between the current minimum of E and a target of B is enormous when measured in the fabric upgrades, services replacements, and capital expenditure required. This is not a marginal tightening. It will render a substantial proportion of the existing non-domestic stock unlettable unless landlords act well in advance.

For assessors, the implications are immediate even though the deadline is not. Landlords are already seeking guidance on upgrade pathways, cost-benefit analysis, and phased improvement strategies. The EPC and its accompanying recommendation report become central documents in investment decision-making, lease negotiations, and asset valuation. The quality, accuracy, and defensibility of your assessments carry materially greater consequences when the difference between a B and a C rating determines whether a building can be lawfully let.

This course sets out the confirmed regulatory position as of mid-2026, details the scope and phasing of the requirements, examines the buildings most affected, and focuses on what changes in your day-to-day practice. It does not rehearse the basics of MEES — you know those. It addresses what is different, what is harder, and where the professional risks sit.

The policy landscape has moved quickly and continues to evolve. Some secondary legislation and detailed guidance remain in development. Where specific regulatory provisions are yet to be finalised, this course identifies them as such. The principles, however, are clear, and the direction of travel is not in doubt.

## Section 1: The Regulatory Framework — What Has Changed and the Compliance Timeline

The original non-domestic MEES regulations came into force under the Energy Efficiency (Private Rented Property) (England and Wales) Regulations 2015. From 1 April 2018, it became unlawful to grant a new lease on a non-domestic property with an EPC rating below E. From 1 April 2023, this extended to all existing leases — meaning continued letting of sub-E properties also became prohibited, subject to valid exemptions registered on the PRS Exemptions Register.

The 2031 EPC B target represents a step-change rather than a simple threshold adjustment. The Government's trajectory, confirmed through consultation responses and policy statements, establishes an interim tightening to EPC C before the B requirement takes effect. The phased approach is designed to give landlords a structured compliance pathway:

**Key milestones (as confirmed or proposed — verify against final statutory instruments):**

- **EPC E minimum** — already in force for all non-domestic lettings (new and existing leases) since April 2023
- **EPC C minimum** — expected to apply from 2027/2028 for new leases and subsequently for existing leases, targeting the broader non-domestic PRS
- **EPC B minimum** — confirmed as the 2031 target for larger non-domestic properties in scope

The scope of the 2031 B requirement focuses on larger non-domestic buildings in the private rented sector. The precise size threshold and the metric used to define "larger" (whether by floor area, rateable value, or another criterion) should be confirmed against the final regulations, as definitions were still under consultation refinement at the time of writing. The direction is clear: the Government is prioritising the highest-emitting, highest-impact buildings first.

The cost cap mechanism — which under the existing regime limits landlord expenditure obligations — is expected to be revised. The current £3,500 (inclusive of VAT) cost cap for non-domestic properties has been widely criticised as too low to drive meaningful improvements. Consultation proposals indicated a substantial increase, potentially linked to property value or a fixed higher figure. The cost cap directly affects exemption eligibility, so any change materially alters the proportion of buildings where landlords can claim an "all improvements made" exemption.

Assessors must track the final statutory instruments carefully. The difference between a commencement date of 2027 versus 2028 for the C requirement, or the precise scope definition for the B requirement, will determine which clients need immediate action.

## Section 2: Which Buildings Are Affected and the Exemptions Landscape

Understanding scope is critical to advising landlords correctly. The MEES regulations apply exclusively to privately rented non-domestic properties in England and Wales that are legally required to have an EPC. This includes offices, retail units, industrial premises, warehouses, and mixed-use buildings where the non-domestic element is separately let. It does not apply to owner-occupied buildings, properties let on certain excluded tenancy types, or buildings that fall outside EPC requirements entirely (such as listed buildings where compliance would unacceptably alter their character, places of worship, temporary structures, and certain industrial sites).

**Scope for the 2031 EPC B target specifically:**

The EPC B requirement applies to larger non-domestic properties. The rationale is straightforward: larger buildings account for disproportionate energy consumption and carbon emissions. They are also more likely to have landlords with the financial capacity to undertake significant upgrades. Smaller non-domestic properties may remain subject to the EPC C requirement rather than B, though assessors should verify this against the final regulations.

**The exemptions framework:**

The existing MEES exemption categories are expected to continue in adapted form:

- **All improvements made exemption** — the landlord has carried out all relevant improvements up to the cost cap and the property still does not reach the required rating. This is the most commonly registered exemption. With a higher cost cap, fewer landlords will qualify.
- **Wall insulation exemption** — where a required wall insulation measure would have a negative impact on the fabric or structure. Relevant for older commercial stock.
- **Consent exemption** — where a third party (superior landlord, planning authority, tenant) has refused consent for required works.
- **Devaluation exemption** — where an independent surveyor determines that the relevant improvements would reduce the property's market value by more than a specified percentage.
- **New landlord exemption** — a temporary exemption (typically six months) for newly registered landlords on properties that became non-compliant before acquisition.

Each exemption must be registered on the PRS Exemptions Register with supporting evidence. Exemptions are time-limited (currently five years) and must be renewed.

For assessors, the critical point is this: the recommendation report attached to your EPC is the document that landlords and their advisors will use to identify which improvements are needed, estimate costs, and determine whether the cost cap has been reached. If your recommendations are incomplete, inaccurate, or poorly costed, you are directly undermining the landlord's ability to plan compliance or legitimately claim an exemption. The professional stakes are higher under a B target than they ever were under E.

## Section 3: Impact on Non-Domestic EPC Methodology and Rating Sensitivity

The shift to an EPC B target exposes sensitivities in the non-domestic EPC methodology that were largely academic when the minimum was E. When you needed to distinguish between an E and an F, the margin of error in most assessments was manageable. Distinguishing between a B and a C — the boundary that will determine lettability — demands greater precision.

Non-domestic EPCs in England and Wales are produced using SBEM (Simplified Building Energy Model) or, for more complex buildings, DSM (Dynamic Simulation Modelling) via approved software. SBEM is the default tool for most assessors. Its outputs depend heavily on input accuracy across several categories:

**Building fabric:**
U-values for walls, roofs, floors, and glazing must be determined as accurately as possible. Default values in SBEM are deliberately conservative — they assume poorer performance than may actually exist. For a building sitting near the B/C boundary, the difference between using a default U-value and an evidenced U-value (from design documentation, building regulations submissions, or on-site measurement) can shift the rating by one or more bands. Assessors should be actively seeking evidence rather than defaulting.

**HVAC systems:**
Heating, ventilation, and air conditioning system specification is frequently the single largest determinant of the non-domestic EPC rating. The type of system, its efficiency, age, and control strategy all feed into SBEM. Misidentifying a system type — recording a variable refrigerant flow system as a split system, for example, or failing to identify the presence of heat recovery on ventilation — can materially distort the output. As more buildings approach the B threshold through targeted HVAC upgrades, getting this right becomes non-negotiable.

**Lighting:**
Lighting energy is a substantial component of the non-domestic calculation. LED retrofits are among the most common and cost-effective improvement measures. Accurately recording lamp types, control gear, and occupancy/daylight controls is essential. An assessment that records generic fluorescent lighting throughout when the building has been partially or fully retrofitted with LEDs will understate performance.

**Renewables and on-site generation:**
Solar PV, CHP, and other on-site generation significantly affect ratings. SBEM handles these through specific input screens, and omitting or incorrectly sizing an installation is an obvious source of error.

**The practical message:** under a B target, the assessor's evidence-gathering at survey stage must be more rigorous. You cannot rely on conservative defaults and produce a defensible assessment. Where a building is within striking distance of B, every input matters. This is not about gaming the system — it is about accuracy. An artificially low rating is as problematic as an artificially high one: it misleads the landlord about upgrade requirements and costs.

Assessors should also be aware that the modelling assumptions within SBEM (activity databases, occupancy patterns, weather data) are periodically updated. Changes to the underlying methodology or the asset rating benchmarks against which EPC bands are calibrated could shift band boundaries. Stay current with DESNZ publications and approved software update notes.

## Section 4: Advising Landlords — Upgrade Pathways and the Assessor's Role in Practice

Your role does not end at lodgement. Increasingly, the EPC and its recommendation report function as a roadmap for capital investment. Landlords, property managers, and commercial agents are using your output to plan retrofit programmes, negotiate green lease clauses, and assess portfolio risk. Under the 2031 framework, this advisory dimension intensifies.

**Common upgrade pathways from C/D to B:**

For a typical non-domestic building currently rated C or low B, the most impactful measures generally include:

- **HVAC replacement or optimisation** — moving from older gas-fired systems or low-efficiency electric heating to high-efficiency heat pumps, modern VRF systems, or enhanced controls. This is often the single measure with the greatest rating impact, but also the highest capital cost.
- **Lighting retrofit** — full LED conversion with appropriate controls (PIR, daylight dimming, absence detection). Often the best cost-to-impact ratio and frequently the first measure implemented.
- **Building fabric improvements** — secondary or replacement glazing, roof insulation, draught-stripping. Impact varies significantly with building type. Fabric measures alone rarely shift a non-domestic rating from C to B.
- **Renewable energy installation** — rooftop solar PV where structural capacity and orientation allow. Effective at improving ratings but dependent on available roof area and planning constraints.
- **Building management system upgrades** — improved controls, zoning, and scheduling. These can influence the EPC rating through SBEM inputs for HVAC and lighting controls, though the effect is sometimes modest in the modelled output versus real-world savings.

**Practical considerations for assessors:**

Be precise in your recommendation report. Generic recommendations ("improve insulation") are unhelpful. Specify the measure, the element it applies to, the indicative impact on the rating, and — where possible — a realistic cost range. Landlords making investment decisions based on vague recommendations will rightly question the value of the assessment.

Be honest about limitations. If a building cannot realistically reach B without a measure that is technically infeasible or disproportionately expensive (full facade replacement on a 1970s concrete-framed office, for example), say so. This information is essential for exemption applications.

Be aware of the interaction between MEES compliance and other regulatory drivers — the Building Safety Act, Part L of the Building Regulations on major renovation, and local planning policies on embodied carbon. Landlords are navigating multiple overlapping requirements, and assessors who understand the broader context provide materially more useful advice.

Finally, document your survey thoroughly. Photography, system nameplate data, construction evidence, and contemporaneous notes protect you professionally and support the credibility of your EPC if it is challenged or relied upon in an exemption application.

## Key Takeaways

- The 2031 EPC B target for larger non-domestic PRS properties is the most significant tightening of MEES since the framework's inception — it will render a large proportion of the current stock non-compliant without intervention
- A phased compliance pathway through EPC C (expected 2027/2028) precedes the B requirement — landlords need to be planning now, not waiting for the deadline
- The cost cap is expected to increase substantially, reducing the proportion of landlords who can claim an "all improvements made" exemption without significant expenditure
- Accuracy at the B/C rating boundary is critical — conservative SBEM defaults that were acceptable under an E minimum are no longer adequate practice
- HVAC system specification and lighting are the highest-impact input categories for most non-domestic EPCs; errors here have direct consequences for compliance determinations
- Recommendation reports must be specific, realistic, and costed — they are now functional investment planning documents, not afterthoughts
- Assessors should document surveys comprehensively; your EPC may be relied upon in exemption applications, lease negotiations, and asset transactions where professional liability exposure is real

## Self-Assessment Questions

**1. Under the current (pre-2031) MEES framework, what is the minimum EPC rating required for all non-domestic private rented properties in England and Wales?**

A) EPC D
B) EPC E
C) EPC C
D) EPC F

**Correct answer: B** — Since 1 April 2023, the minimum EPC rating for all non-domestic PRS lettings (new and existing leases) has been E under the Energy Efficiency (Private Rented Property) (England and Wales) Regulations 2015.

---

**2. Which of the following is the most likely single highest-impact measure for improving a non-domestic EPC rating from C to B?**

A) Draught-stripping external doors
B) Installing cavity wall insulation
C) Replacing the HVAC system with a high-efficiency alternative
D) Adding secondary glazing to single-glazed windows

**Correct answer: C** — HVAC system type and efficiency is typically the dominant factor in non-domestic EPC calculations via SBEM. While all listed measures contribute, HVAC replacement generally produces the greatest rating improvement for buildings near the B/C boundary.

---

**3. What is the maximum duration of a registered MEES exemption under the current framework?**

A) 3 years
B) 12 months
C) 5 years
D) 10 years

**Correct answer: C** — MEES exemptions registered on the PRS Exemptions Register are valid for five years, after which the landlord must reassess compliance or re-register an exemption with fresh evidence.

---

**4. When producing a non-domestic EPC for a building near the B/C rating boundary, which approach to U-value inputs is most appropriate?**

A) Use SBEM default values throughout for consistency
B) Use evidenced U-values from building documentation or measurement where available, defaulting only where no evidence exists
C) Use the most favourable U-values available to maximise the rating
D) Omit U-value inputs and allow the software to auto-populate

**Correct answer: B** — Assessors should use the best available evidence for building fabric performance. Default values in SBEM are conservative and may understate actual performance. However, evidence must be genuine and documented — using unjustified favourable values is equally inappropriate.

---

**5. Which of the following property types is NOT within scope of the non-domestic MEES regulations?**

A) A privately rented high-street retail unit with a valid EPC
B) An owner-occupied office building
C) A privately rented industrial warehouse with a valid EPC
D) A privately rented serviced office suite within a multi-let building

**Correct answer: B** — MEES regulations apply to privately rented properties. Owner-occupied buildings are outside scope regardless of their EPC rating or energy performance.
