

# Future Homes Standard 2025: What Energy Assessors Need to Know

## Introduction

The Future Homes Standard represents the most significant shift in the energy performance framework for new housing in England since Part L was first introduced. Following years of consultation—the 2019 initial consultation, the 2023 consultation on the full standard, and the government's definitive response published in March 2026—we now have a settled regulatory picture. The days of speculative interpretation are over. What remains is implementation, and that falls squarely on our desks.

The headline figures are substantial. New homes built to the FHS will be required to produce 75–80% less carbon dioxide emissions compared to the Part L 2013 baseline. Fossil fuel combustion heating—gas boilers, oil boilers—will no longer be permitted in new dwellings. The notional dwelling specification under the revised Approved Document L has been comprehensively rewritten, with tighter fabric U-values, enhanced airtightness expectations, and a shift toward heat pumps as the default low-carbon heating technology. Ventilation strategy, thermal bridging, and overheating risk all receive more granular treatment.

For working assessors, this is not merely a regulatory update to be filed and forgotten. It changes what you model, how you model it, and what you advise. SAP 10.2 (or its successor methodology as confirmed) underpins the new calculations, with revised carbon emission factors that reflect the ongoing decarbonisation of the electricity grid. That single change alone reorders the relative carbon impact of heating technologies and dramatically shifts the EPC landscape.

If you are lodging EPCs for new-build plots, you will encounter the new notional dwelling and its compliance metrics imminently. If you work in retrofit, you need to understand the direction of travel: the FHS sets the benchmark toward which the existing stock will increasingly be measured, and it will shape client expectations, funding criteria, and future regulatory standards for existing homes.

This course sets out the framework as confirmed, addresses the practical modelling and compliance implications, and identifies what you need to do now to prepare.

## Section 1: The Regulatory Framework — What Has Actually Been Confirmed

The government's March 2026 response to the Future Homes Standard consultation confirmed the regulatory architecture that had been signalled, with some important clarifications and a handful of revisions from the 2023 proposals.

**The Core Mechanism**

The FHS operates through amendments to the Building Regulations 2010, specifically through a revised Approved Document L (Conservation of Fuel and Power), Volume 1: Dwellings. The compliance methodology continues to use the Target Emission Rate (TER) and Target Primary Energy Rate (TPER) approach, where a proposed dwelling must demonstrate performance equal to or better than a notional dwelling built to the new reference specification. The Target Fabric Energy Efficiency (TFEE) rate also remains as a backstop to prevent over-reliance on services at the expense of building fabric.

**Transitional Arrangements**

The transitional provisions are critical for assessors working on developments currently in the pipeline. The confirmed approach uses a building work commencement model: dwellings where building work commences (meaning substantive work, not simply groundworks or site clearance) before the implementation date may be completed under the current Part L 2021 standards, subject to a defined completion window. Assessors should verify the precise commencement date and completion window as confirmed in the statutory instrument, as these dates have practical consequences for which SAP version and notional dwelling specification apply to a given plot.

**Interaction with Devolved Administrations**

The FHS applies to England only. Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland continue to operate under their own building standards regimes, each at different stages of tightening energy performance requirements. Assessors working across borders must track these separately—there is no single UK-wide standard despite the shared ambition of decarbonisation.

**The End of the Interim Uplift**

The 2021 Part L uplift (the so-called "interim uplift" requiring approximately 31% CO₂ reduction against Part L 2013) was always positioned as a stepping stone. The FHS replaces it definitively. The notional dwelling specification that assessors have been using since June 2022 is superseded in its entirety for dwellings falling under the new regulations.

## Section 2: Fabric Performance and Heating — The New Technical Benchmarks

The FHS notional dwelling specification establishes the reference performance levels against which proposed dwellings are assessed. While individual designs may trade between elements provided overall compliance is met, the notional values signal the expected standard and set the practical floor for design quality.

**Fabric U-Values**

The confirmed notional dwelling fabric specification represents a meaningful tightening from Part L 2021. Expected notional U-values include enhanced performance for walls, floors, and roofs relative to the 2021 notional dwelling. The precise figures are set out in the revised Approved Document L tables, and assessors should ensure they are working from the published version rather than consultation-stage proposals, as some values were adjusted in the final response.

For glazing, the notional specification assumes high-performance double or triple glazing. Assessors should note that window energy performance interacts with solar gain, daylight, and overheating risk—a point reinforced by the principles set out in [L38] BS 6262-2: Glazing for Buildings: Energy, Light and Sound, which addresses the balance between thermal performance, solar energy transmittance, and light transmission. The trade-offs between g-values, U-values, and light transmittance become more consequential under a standard that simultaneously tightens fabric requirements and strengthens overheating mitigation under Part O.

**Airtightness**

The notional dwelling airtightness has been tightened from the 2021 specification. This reinforces the trend toward higher-performance envelopes and places greater emphasis on accurate pressure testing and robust detailing at design stage. For assessors, this means closer scrutiny of as-built test results and their alignment with design-stage assumptions entered in SAP.

**The Heating System Prohibition**

The most publicly visible element of the FHS is the effective prohibition of fossil fuel heating in new dwellings. The notional dwelling is modelled with an air source heat pump, and the carbon and primary energy metrics are calibrated such that a gas or oil boiler cannot achieve compliance except in a narrow range of exceptional circumstances (and even then, the government has signalled that these will be tightly constrained).

This is not simply a notional assumption—it represents a functional ban. Developers may select alternative low-carbon heating technologies (ground source heat pumps, heat networks meeting defined carbon intensity thresholds, direct electric heating in limited circumstances where fabric performance is exceptionally high), but the gas boiler era for new homes is over.

**Implications for SAP Modelling**

The revised carbon emission factors in SAP 10.2 reflect the decarbonising electricity grid. Electricity's emission factor has dropped substantially compared to SAP 2012, fundamentally changing the relative performance of electric versus gas heating in the calculation. This is not a policy preference embedded in the methodology—it is an evidence-based update reflecting actual grid carbon intensity and projections.

## Section 3: EPC Assessments, Compliance Routes, and the SAP Calculation Landscape

The FHS changes the practical workflow for assessors conducting new-build EPC assessments in several important respects.

**SAP Version and Software**

The FHS is underpinned by SAP 10.2 (or the confirmed successor version at implementation). Assessors must ensure they are using approved software versions that incorporate the updated methodology, emission factors, fuel prices, and notional dwelling specification. Running a new-build assessment on legacy software or an outdated SAP version will produce non-compliant results and unlodgeable certificates.

Software providers have been updating their platforms in line with the approved specification, but assessors should verify that their tool of choice has received formal BEIS/DESNZ approval for use under the new regulations before lodging. This is not a theoretical risk—every major Part L transition has produced a window where software versions overlap, and errors in this period can create significant downstream liability.

**The Compliance Metrics**

The FHS retains the three-metric compliance structure:

1. **Dwelling Emission Rate (DER) ≤ Target Emission Rate (TER):** The carbon metric, now more heavily influenced by the updated grid electricity factor.
2. **Dwelling Primary Energy Rate (DPER) ≤ Target Primary Energy Rate (TPER):** The primary energy metric, which captures total energy resource use.
3. **Dwelling Fabric Energy Efficiency (DFEE) ≥ Target Fabric Energy Efficiency (TFEE):** The fabric backstop, ensuring the building envelope performs to a minimum standard independent of services.

All three must be met. This is unchanged in principle from Part L 2021, but the target levels have tightened substantially.

**As-Built Versus Design Stage**

Assessors producing design-stage SAP calculations for Building Control submissions must be alert to the margin between design intent and as-built performance. Under the FHS, where fabric tolerances are tighter and heating system efficiency assumptions are more precisely defined, small deviations—an underperforming pressure test result, a thermal bridge detail that doesn't match the accredited value used at design stage, a heat pump installed with a lower SCOP than modelled—can tip a plot from compliant to non-compliant.

The as-built EPC is the definitive assessment. Assessors working with developers need to communicate clearly that FHS compliance margins are narrower than previous standards and that site quality assurance is no longer a nice-to-have—it is a compliance necessity.

**Photovoltaics and Renewables**

The notional dwelling includes a PV array. The inclusion of renewables in the notional specification means that a dwelling without PV (or equivalent renewable contribution) will need to exceed the notional fabric and services performance to compensate. In practice, the FHS has been calibrated on the assumption that most new homes will include rooftop PV. This has implications for plot orientation, roof design, and shading analysis within the SAP assessment.

## Section 4: Practical Implications — Workflow, Client Advisory, and Retrofit Context

**What Changes in Your Day-to-Day Work**

If you assess new-build dwellings, the immediate changes are:

- **Software and methodology:** Confirm you are using the approved SAP version and software build for FHS compliance. Check your licence and update schedule.
- **Notional dwelling specification:** Familiarise yourself thoroughly with the new reference values. The notional dwelling has changed in fabric, services, renewables, and ventilation assumptions. If you are still mentally benchmarking against Part L 2021 notional values, your intuition about what will and will not comply is now unreliable.
- **Heat pump literacy:** You will be modelling heat pumps as the default heating system. Understand SCOP, flow temperatures, emitter sizing, and how these interact within SAP. If your experience is predominantly gas-boiler housing, invest time in understanding heat pump system design at a level sufficient to interrogate the specifications you are given by M&E designers.
- **Thermal bridging and airtightness:** These are no longer marginal factors. They can determine compliance. Ensure you are correctly applying accredited construction details or calculated psi-values and that you understand the consequences of default values versus bespoke calculations.

**Client Advisory and Retrofit**

For assessors working primarily in existing housing and retrofit, the FHS does not directly apply to existing buildings. However, its influence is pervasive:

- Clients increasingly reference FHS standards when discussing retrofit ambitions or future-proofing.
- Funding schemes and local authority programmes are beginning to benchmark against FHS fabric performance levels.
- The direction of travel for SAP and RdSAP updates (including the anticipated RdSAP successor methodology) is toward alignment with FHS-era carbon factors and performance expectations.

Understand the FHS not as a regulation you must enforce on existing stock, but as the reference point that defines "good" in the current policy environment. When a homeowner or landlord asks what standard they should be aiming for, the FHS gives you a technically grounded answer.

**Competency and CPD**

The FHS implementation will likely trigger updates to assessor competency frameworks and scheme requirements. Stay ahead of this. If your scheme operator announces additional training or assessment requirements linked to FHS, treat them as a priority rather than a box-ticking exercise.

## Key Takeaways

- The Future Homes Standard is confirmed and represents a 75–80% carbon reduction over Part L 2013, with fossil fuel heating effectively prohibited in new dwellings in England.
- The notional dwelling specification under the revised Approved Document L has been comprehensively rewritten—assessors must update their working knowledge of all reference values.
- SAP 10.2 (or the confirmed successor) with updated carbon emission factors is the mandated calculation methodology; ensure your software is approved and current before lodging any FHS-era assessment.
- All three compliance metrics (DER/TER, DPER/TPER, DFEE/TFEE) must be met, and the tighter targets mean compliance margins are narrower than under Part L 2021.
- Heat pump system design literacy is now a core competency for new-build assessors—understand SCOP, flow temperatures, and emitter interactions within SAP.
- Glazing specifications require careful balancing of U-value, g-value, and light transmittance, informed by principles in [L38] BS 6262-2, particularly given the concurrent requirements of Part O overheating mitigation.
- Retrofit assessors should understand the FHS as the benchmark defining current policy expectations, even though it does not directly regulate existing buildings.

## Self-Assessment Questions

**1. Under the Future Homes Standard, what is the approximate CO₂ emission reduction target for new dwellings compared to the Part L 2013 baseline?**

A) 31%
B) 50%
C) 75–80%
D) 100% (net zero carbon)

**Correct answer: C**
*The FHS targets a 75–80% reduction in CO₂ emissions compared to Part L 2013. Option A reflects the 2021 interim uplift, not the full FHS.*

---

**2. Which heating system does the FHS notional dwelling specification assume as default?**

A) Gas condensing boiler
B) Direct electric panel heaters
C) Air source heat pump
D) Biomass boiler

**Correct answer: C**
*The notional dwelling assumes an air source heat pump. The FHS effectively prohibits fossil fuel heating in new dwellings, and the notional specification is calibrated around heat pump performance.*

---

**3. Under the FHS compliance framework, how many performance metrics must a proposed dwelling satisfy?**

A) One (DER ≤ TER only)
B) Two (DER ≤ TER and DPER ≤ TPER)
C) Three (DER ≤ TER, DPER ≤ TPER, and DFEE ≥ TFEE)
D) Four (DER, DPER, DFEE, and a separate overheating metric under Part L)

**Correct answer: C**
*Three metrics must be satisfied: the carbon emission rate, the primary energy rate, and the fabric energy efficiency rate. Overheating is addressed under Part O, not as a Part L compliance metric.*

---

**4. When considering glazing specifications under the FHS, which British Standard provides guidance on balancing energy performance, light transmittance, and solar gain?**

A) BS 5250
B) BS 6262-2
C) BS 9250
D) BS 8206-1

**Correct answer: B**
*[L38] BS 6262-2: Glazing for Buildings: Energy, Light and Sound addresses the interaction between thermal performance, solar energy transmittance, and light transmission—all relevant to FHS compliance and Part O overheating risk.*

---

**5. A developer commences building work on a housing site before the FHS implementation date. Which standard applies to those dwellings?**

A) The Future Homes Standard, regardless of commencement date
B) Part L 2021, subject to a defined completion window under transitional provisions
C) Part L 2013, as the original baseline standard
D) No energy performance standard, as the site is exempt during transition

**Correct answer: B**
*Transitional provisions allow dwellings where substantive building work commenced before the implementation date to be completed under Part L 2021, provided they fall within the confirmed completion window. Assessors must verify the precise dates in the statutory instrument.*
