

# Thermal Bridging in Retrofit Assessment: What Assessors Must Record
**Course ID:** MER-RA-010 | **CPD Hours:** 1.0 | **Published:** March 2026

## Learning Objectives

- Explain what thermal bridging is, why it matters in the context of retrofit assessment, and how it is characterised by linear thermal transmittance (psi values).
- Identify the common junction types in domestic construction where thermal bridges occur and record their presence and condition as required by PAS 2035:2023.
- Apply the correct approach to thermal bridging data in the Retrofit Assessment — distinguishing between the assessor's data collection role and the designer's thermal bridge mitigation role.
- Recognise the moisture and mould risk implications of thermal bridges and record them as part of the moisture risk assessment.
- Understand how thermal bridge data from the assessment feeds into retrofit design and how unmitigated bridges affect post-retrofit performance.

---

## Introduction

Thermal bridging is one of the less visible but highly consequential aspects of building energy performance. A thermal bridge is any location in the building fabric where the rate of heat flow is significantly higher than through the surrounding insulated element — a weak point in the thermal envelope where heat escapes disproportionately.

In the context of retrofit, thermal bridges matter for two reasons. First, they account for a significant portion of total fabric heat loss in many properties — particularly older housing where construction junctions were designed without thermal break considerations. Second, they create cold surface temperatures at their internal face, which promotes condensation, mould growth, and the occupant health impacts associated with both. Insulating the main wall area while leaving thermal bridges unaddressed can make the bridge relatively worse — the proportion of total heat loss attributable to bridges increases as the main element is improved.

PAS 2035:2023 requires the Retrofit Assessor to record thermal bridging characteristics as part of the dwelling data collection. This course explains what needs to be recorded, how to identify the common bridge types on site, and how the assessment data flows into the design process.

## Section 1: What Thermal Bridges Are and Why They Matter in Retrofit

A thermal bridge occurs wherever the thermal continuity of the insulation layer is interrupted. The interruption can be structural (a steel beam or concrete column penetrating the insulated plane), geometric (a corner, where surface area changes), or material (a mortar joint, a wall tie, or a balcony slab connecting the heated interior to the exterior).

**Linear thermal transmittance (Psi value).** The magnitude of a thermal bridge is expressed as a linear thermal transmittance, or Psi (Ψ) value, in W/m·K. The higher the Psi value, the more heat flows through the bridge per metre of its length. Psi values for common construction junctions range from approximately 0.02 W/m·K (a well-insulated cavity wall-to-party wall junction) to 0.5 W/m·K or more (an uninsulated steel balcony or concrete ground floor slab extending through the wall to an uninsulated external face). The total bridging heat loss for a junction is the Psi value multiplied by the length of the junction in the dwelling.

**The Y-factor in RdSAP.** In RdSAP 10 (and its predecessor RdSAP 9.94), thermal bridging is accounted for through the Y-factor — a global addition to the calculated heat loss that represents the aggregate contribution of all junctions and repeating bridges. RdSAP 10 uses a default Y-factor based on the construction type and age, or allows the use of calculated junction Psi values where these are available. The default Y-factor for older construction (pre-1990s, without specific thermal break detailing) is significantly higher than for post-2006 construction. Incorrect age band recording, or failure to identify insulation continuity interruptions, propagates directly into the Y-factor and the calculated energy demand.

**The retrofit implication.** When fabric insulation is added to a wall, the thermal bridge at junctions becomes relatively more significant because the heat loss through the main wall area is reduced. An uninsulated concrete lintel above a window aperture in an uninsulated solid wall may contribute 5% of total wall heat loss. After IWI to the main wall area, the same lintel may contribute 20% of the remaining wall heat loss. Retrofit design that addresses the main element without addressing the junctions leaves significant heat loss and cold-surface risk unmitigated.

## Section 2: Junction Types — Identification and Recording

**Wall-to-floor junction (ground floor).** The junction between the external wall and the ground floor is one of the most thermally significant and most commonly unmitigated in existing housing. In cavity masonry construction, the wall cavity typically terminates at the ground slab, creating an uninsulated pathway between the external and internal faces. In solid masonry construction, the wall runs continuously from foundation to first floor, with no thermal break. The condition to record: construction type at the perimeter, whether any edge insulation is visible (it will typically be visible at the DPC level if present as a strip of insulation board in the cavity), and any visible cold spot or damp evidence at the internal skirting/wall junction.

**Wall-to-roof junction (eaves/verge).** At the eaves, the roof insulation and wall insulation (where present) must be continuous for the thermal envelope to be maintained. In traditional cavity wall construction with loft insulation at ceiling joist level, the insulation typically extends to the eaves but may be compressed or absent at the very edge. The assessor should note the eaves detail visible from the loft (where accessible) and any evidence of cold surface or mould at the top of the external wall internally.

**Reveals (window and door openings).** The reveal — the exposed wall between the outer face of the window frame and the inner face of the room — is a thermal bridge in almost all domestic construction. In solid-wall properties, the reveal is full-thickness uninsulated masonry. In cavity wall properties, the cavity is closed at the reveal, creating a direct thermal pathway. Reveals are frequently visible indicators of bridging — they are the location of "cold corners" in rooms, and mould growth at reveals is a diagnostic indicator. The assessor records: reveal depth (approximate), reveal construction (insulated return or uninsulated masonry), and any mould or condensation evidence at reveal surfaces.

**Lintels and structural elements.** Concrete lintels over openings are one of the most common significant thermal bridges in 20th-century domestic construction. A concrete lintel with no thermal break from the external to the internal face creates a direct conductive pathway. Steel lintels, while structurally efficient, have even higher conductivity. The assessor records: lintel material (visible from the reveal or from any accessible soffit), approximate length (governed by opening width), and whether any insulation to the lintel is apparent (a warm lintel surface on a cold day is indicative of insulation; a cold, condensation-prone lintel surface indicates bridging).

**Party walls.** The junction between the external wall and the party wall is a thermal bridge at the inner leaf of the cavity (where the party wall connects to the external wall leaf). In post-2006 construction this junction is typically designed with a thermal break; in older construction it is not. The assessor notes the construction type and age as a proxy for party wall thermal bridge treatment.

## Section 3: Cold Surfaces, Mould Risk, and the Assessment Record

Thermal bridges create cold surface temperatures on the internal face of the building fabric. The critical threshold is the dew point of the internal air — when the surface temperature falls below the dew point, moisture condenses. In dwellings where thermal bridges are significant, this condensation occurs at the coldest surfaces first: reveals, external wall corners, ground floor perimeter, and at lintel soffits.

**Recording cold surface evidence.** The assessor should record any visible evidence of condensation, efflorescence (salt deposits from cyclic wetting and drying), or mould growth at junction locations. The location should be specific: "black mould at the skirting board on the east external wall in the living room" is actionable; "some mould present" is not. The pattern of mould — whether it corresponds to known bridge locations — is itself a diagnostic. Mould precisely at the perimeter of the ground floor or at window reveals, in a dwelling with otherwise adequate ventilation, points strongly to bridging as the primary cause.

**Distinguishing bridging from ventilation failure.** Both inadequate ventilation and thermal bridges cause surface condensation and mould. The diagnostic distinction: bridging-driven mould is typically localised at specific junction locations (corners, reveals, lintels), while ventilation-failure mould is more widespread and associated with wet rooms, north-facing surfaces, and behind furniture. In practice, many dwellings have both issues — the assessment must record all observed mould locations with sufficient positional detail for the designer to distinguish causes.

## Section 4: Thermal Bridge Data in the Design Process

The Retrofit Assessor's thermal bridge data feeds into the retrofit design in several ways:

**Targeted bridge mitigation.** Where significant thermal bridges are identified at specific junctions (concrete lintel, uninsulated reveal, uninsulated ground floor perimeter), the designer may specify targeted mitigation: insulated lintel casing within IWI, reveal insulation boards, edge insulation at ground floor. The assessment data tells the designer which junctions are present and whether evidence suggests they are thermally significant.

**Fabric U-value calculation.** Where the Retrofit Assessment feeds into a SAP calculation or a retrofit design calculation (for projects using full SAP rather than RdSAP), accurate junction recording allows the use of calculated Psi values rather than default Y-factors. This typically produces a more accurate (and often better) calculated energy performance, justifying a more efficient heating system specification.

**Preventing post-retrofit performance gaps.** The most common form of post-retrofit disappointment is a dwelling whose actual energy use is significantly higher than the design calculation predicted. Unmitigated thermal bridges are one of the primary causes of this gap. Where the assessment records significant bridging at key junctions, the designer can address it in the specification, and the Retrofit Coordinator can commission appropriate monitoring to verify post-retrofit performance.

**The assessor's limiting principle.** The assessor records junction types, their presence, and evidence of their thermal and moisture impact. The assessor does not calculate Psi values, does not determine which bridges should be mitigated, and does not specify bridge-mitigation details. These are design-stage functions. An assessment that records "multiple thermal bridges present, recommend insulated reveals" has exceeded the assessor's scope.

## Key Takeaways

- Thermal bridges are persistent heat loss and cold-surface risk points at construction junctions; they become relatively more significant after main-element insulation is added.
- The four most important junction types to record in domestic retrofit assessment are: wall-to-floor (ground floor perimeter), wall-to-roof (eaves), reveals (window and door openings), and lintels.
- Mould and condensation at junction-specific locations is diagnostic of thermal bridging and must be recorded with precise location data, not as a general observation.
- The assessor records junction presence, visible condition, and evidence of impact — Psi value calculation and bridge mitigation specification are designer functions.
- Unaddressed thermal bridges are a leading cause of post-retrofit performance gaps — assessment data that clearly identifies them is the starting point for addressing them in design.
- Thermal bridge data from the assessment can support the use of calculated Psi values (rather than default Y-factors) in SAP calculations, improving design accuracy.

## Self-Assessment Questions

**Q1.** Black mould is observed precisely at the skirting board level along the entire external north wall of a ground floor living room, but not on any other wall surface. Ventilation assessment shows adequate trickle vents and functional extract fans. What is the most likely cause?

A) Ventilation failure — the north wall receives insufficient air movement
B) Thermal bridging at the wall-to-ground-floor junction, creating a cold surface at the perimeter where dew point is reached
C) Penetrating damp through the north wall
D) Rising damp — the DPC has failed

**Correct answer: B**

*Localised mould at skirting board level along the full external wall, in a dwelling with adequate ventilation, is the classic indicator of thermal bridging at the wall-to-floor junction. The cold perimeter surface (where the external wall meets the uninsulated ground slab) falls below dew point in cold weather, causing surface condensation and mould.*

---

**Q2.** A 1975 cavity masonry property has concrete lintels over all window and door openings. The assessor observes cold, damp soffit surfaces under the lintels in the living room windows during a winter survey. What must be recorded?

A) Nothing — concrete lintels are standard in this construction period
B) The windows are draughty — the damp soffits indicate infiltration, not bridging
C) The assessor should only record this if mould is visibly present
D) The concrete lintel type, approximate length, and the observed cold/damp condition at the soffit — flagging thermal bridging at the lintel locations as an assessment finding

**Correct answer: D**

*Cold, damp surfaces under concrete lintels during cold weather are indicative of thermal bridging at the lintel. The assessor records the lintel material, length (approximate, from opening width), and the observed surface condition. This data informs the designer's decision about targeted lintel insulation as part of any IWI or EWI specification.*

---

**Q3.** A Retrofit Assessment records "thermal bridges present at window reveals throughout." The designer requests more specific data. What information is missing?

A) The external orientation of each reveal
B) The thermal conductivity of the masonry
C) The specific location of each reveal with an observed problem (which rooms, which windows), the reveal depth (approximate), the reveal construction (insulated return or uninsulated masonry), and any mould or condensation evidence at each location
D) The window glass specification

**Correct answer: C**

*"Thermal bridges present at window reveals throughout" is not actionable data. The designer needs: specific locations (room-by-room, window-by-window for significant cases), reveal depth and construction type, and evidence of impact (mould, condensation) at each. Precise, location-referenced data is what enables targeted bridge mitigation design.*

---

**Q4.** What is the Y-factor in RdSAP 10, and how does thermal bridging data from the Retrofit Assessment affect it?

A) The Y-factor is a global coefficient representing the aggregate heat loss from all thermal bridges and junctions. Where assessed junction Psi values are available from detailed survey data, they can replace the default Y-factor with a more accurate calculation
B) The Y-factor is only used in full SAP, not in retrofit assessments
C) The Y-factor is the air permeability measure — ventilation data affects it
D) The Y-factor is the thermal transmittance of the roof element — accurate roof insulation data improves it

**Correct answer: A**

*The Y-factor is the calculated or defaulted addition to heat loss representing all thermal junctions. Default Y-factors (applied when no junction-specific data is available) are typically conservative. Where the Retrofit Assessment includes detailed junction characterisation, calculated Psi values can be used in a more refined calculation, often producing a better (lower) Y-factor than the age-band default.*

---

**Q5.** An assessor writes in the Retrofit Assessment: "Concrete lintels over all openings — recommend insulated lintel casing be specified as part of the IWI package." What is wrong with this statement?

A) The recommendation is acceptable because it is flagged as a recommendation rather than a requirement
B) Nothing — the assessor is providing helpful guidance to the designer
C) The assessor can make this recommendation if they also hold a Retrofit Coordinator qualification
D) Specifying "insulated lintel casing as part of the IWI package" is a design determination that exceeds the Retrofit Assessor's scope. The assessor should record the lintel type and evidence of thermal impact; the Retrofit Designer determines whether and how to mitigate the bridge

**Correct answer: D**

*Specifying retrofit measures — even in the form of recommendations — is outside the Retrofit Assessor's scope under PAS 2035:2023. The assessor records data. The Retrofit Designer specifies measures. An assessor who writes measure recommendations introduces liability (what if the recommendation is wrong?) and blurs the role boundary that PAS 2035 is designed to enforce.*
