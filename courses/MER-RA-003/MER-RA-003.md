

# Dwelling Data Collection: PAS 2035:2023 Annex A Requirements

## Learning Objectives

- Identify the mandatory and recommended data fields specified in PAS 2035:2023 Annex A and distinguish between those required for every assessment and those that are condition-dependent.
- Understand how Annex A data fields connect to the downstream retrofit design process, and why gaps in assessment data create problems at the design and installation stages.
- Apply the correct recording conventions for construction data, occupancy data, and energy system data as specified in PAS 2035:2023.
- Recognise the common data collection gaps that lead to TrustMark lodgement rejection and implement site practices to prevent them.
- Explain the TrustMark data submission requirements and the evidence standards that support them.

---

## Introduction

Annex A of PAS 2035:2023 is the specification for what a Retrofit Assessment must contain. It is not a suggestion — it is a normative annex, meaning its requirements are mandatory for compliance. A Retrofit Assessment that does not satisfy Annex A is not a PAS 2035:2023 compliant assessment, regardless of the assessor's qualifications or the quality of their site observations.

The shift to a more structured, mandatory data framework in the 2023 edition was a direct response to a pattern identified across multiple ECO4 and SHDF projects: assessment data packs that were incomplete, inconsistent, or recorded in a format that the Retrofit Coordinator could not use to make design decisions. The result was that design teams made assumptions to fill gaps — and those assumptions, when wrong, produced retrofit specifications that created problems.

This course maps the Annex A requirements, explains why each category of data matters for the downstream process, and identifies the most common gaps that result in TrustMark lodgement rejection. It is a practical working reference for Retrofit Assessors preparing data packs for submission.

## Section 1: Annex A Structure — Mandatory vs Condition-Dependent Fields

Annex A organises the required assessment data into the following main categories:

1. Property identification and tenure
2. Dwelling description and construction
3. Energy systems (heating, hot water, ventilation, renewables)
4. Occupancy and health
5. Moisture and environmental conditions
6. Current energy performance (EPC data)
7. Owner/occupier priorities and constraints

Within each category, PAS 2035:2023 distinguishes between:

**Always mandatory fields** — must be recorded for every assessment regardless of property type, condition, or proposed measures. Examples: property address and UPRN, construction type (wall, roof, floor), heating fuel and system type, occupant count, and EPC reference.

**Condition-dependent mandatory fields** — mandatory when a specific condition is present. Examples: the moisture risk indicators (Section 3 of this course) are mandatory where any moisture-sensitive measure is proposed; chimney and fireplace data is mandatory where open-flue appliances are present; rainwater goods detail is mandatory where external fabric measures are proposed.

**Recommended fields** — not mandatory for PAS 2035 compliance but strongly recommended by TrustMark for data quality. Examples: photographs of all elevations, internal surface temperatures at known cold bridge locations, detailed floor construction description. Recommended fields that are systematically omitted will be flagged in TrustMark quality reviews even where they do not constitute a compliance failure.

**The practical implication.** You cannot pre-determine which fields are relevant before you arrive on site. The condition-dependent mandatory fields are triggered by what you find. An assessor who determines their data collection checklist before the survey will miss condition-dependent fields that are only identifiable on site. The checklist must be comprehensive enough to capture everything, with condition-triggered sections that activate when a relevant feature is identified.

## Section 2: Construction Data — What Must Be Recorded

**Walls.** For each wall type present, Annex A requires: construction type (cavity masonry, solid masonry, timber frame, steel frame, concrete panel, stone), age band, finish (rendered, faced brick, cladding, or other), insulation status and type if present, U-value (calculated, declared, or default with basis stated), and any observed defects relevant to moisture or structural risk. Where more than one wall type is present (e.g., original solid wall to front elevation and 1970s extension with cavity to rear), each must be recorded separately.

**Roof.** Roof type, construction, and insulation status for each roof element. Where a pitched roof with accessible loft is present: insulation depth measured, insulation type, position (at ceiling joist level or rafter level), and U-value. Where a room-in-roof element is present: Type 1 or Type 2 classification, insulation at sloping ceiling and knee walls. Where a flat roof is present: insulation type and position (warm or cold roof configuration). All roof elements must be recorded — a property with a pitched roof over the main body and a flat roof over a rear extension requires separate entries for each.

**Floors.** Ground floor construction type and insulation status for each floor zone. Upper floors above unheated spaces (garages, basements) must be identified and recorded as exposed floor elements. The connection between floor construction and moisture risk at ground level (particularly suspended timber floors with inadequate sub-floor ventilation) must be noted where relevant.

**Windows and doors.** Glazing type, frame type, approximate installation date (and basis for date estimate), condition, and air permeability implications. The Annex A requirements here are less granular than RdSAP 10 window data but require sufficient information to inform draught-stripping decisions and the relationship between window replacement and ventilation strategy.

**Key gap: element boundaries.** One of the most common Annex A deficiencies is incomplete recording of element boundaries — the junctions between wall and floor, wall and roof, window reveals, and party wall interfaces. These are the locations of thermal bridges, moisture ingress pathways, and structural movement. Annex A requires that significant junction defects or abnormalities are recorded. Assessors who complete a thorough central-room inspection but do not examine reveals, skirtings at external walls, and eaves junctions will miss mandatory data.

## Section 3: Energy Systems — Heating, Hot Water and Ventilation Data

**Heating system.** Mandatory fields include: fuel type, primary heating system type (boiler — combi/regular/system; heat pump; direct electric; storage heater; district heat network), manufacturer and model where identifiable, approximate installation year, efficiency (from the PCDB or manufacturer data where available, or age-band estimate), heat emitter type (radiators, underfloor heating, fan convectors, warm air), and controls (programmer, room thermostat, TRVs, weather compensation, load compensation). Secondary heating systems must also be recorded.

The gap most frequently cited by TrustMark in quality reviews: failing to record the heat emitter type and its condition. A property that is proposed for heat pump installation must have its emitter system assessed in detail — radiator sizing relative to the rooms served, flow temperature capability of the existing system, and condition. An assessment that records "gas boiler, year 2010, replaced by heat pump" without emitter data is incomplete and will block the design stage.

**Hot water.** Cylinder type and volume where present (insulated/uninsulated, jacket thickness, thermostat setting), combi boiler confirmation, solar thermal pre-heat confirmation, heat pump DHW operation where relevant. Missing hot water data is a consistent gap in assessments for properties proposed for heat pump or solar thermal installation.

**Ventilation.** Current ventilation strategy for each habitable space: purpose-provided ventilation type (trickle vents, passive stack vents, extract fans — with location and condition), background ventilation adequacy relative to occupancy, and any known history of ventilation problems. This is not a BREEAM or Part F compliance assessment — it is a characterisation of the existing system sufficient to inform decisions about its adequacy after retrofit.

## Section 4: TrustMark Data Submission — What Gets Rejected and Why

TrustMark's Retrofit Assessment data submission requirements build on PAS 2035:2023 Annex A. Data is submitted through the TrustMark data warehouse, and a quality check is applied at submission. The most common rejection reasons, based on TrustMark quality review data:

**1. Missing UPRN.** The Unique Property Reference Number is a mandatory field. It must be the correct UPRN for the specific address — not a neighbouring property's UPRN or a postcode-level approximation. UPRN lookup is available at https://www.findmyaddress.co.uk/ and via Ordnance Survey.

**2. EPC reference missing or incorrect.** The current EPC for the property must be referenced by its RRN (Report Reference Number) from the EPC Register. A current EPC is required within a specified period prior to assessment (check scheme and programme requirements). Where no current EPC exists, one must be lodged before or concurrent with the Retrofit Assessment.

**3. Incomplete construction data.** Wall type recorded without insulation status, or roof type without insulation depth, are the most common partial entries. TrustMark requires complete records for all fabric elements — partial entries are rejected.

**4. Missing moisture risk indicators.** As of PAS 2035:2023, all six moisture-risk indicator fields are mandatory. Where any are absent, the submission is non-compliant.

**5. Occupancy data incomplete.** Occupant count and any health/vulnerability flags are mandatory fields. A blank occupancy section is a rejection trigger.

**6. No photographic evidence.** TrustMark requires a minimum photographic pack to be associated with the assessment record. Specific minimum requirements vary by programme, but all elevations, the boiler data plate, the consumer unit, and the loft space (where accessible) are standard minima.

## Key Takeaways

- Annex A is a normative (mandatory) annex — a Retrofit Assessment that does not satisfy it is not PAS 2035:2023 compliant, regardless of other quality indicators.
- Mandatory fields divide into always-mandatory and condition-dependent — the latter are triggered by what you find on site, so your site checklist must be comprehensive enough to capture conditions before they trigger.
- Element boundary recording (reveals, junctions, skirtings at external walls, eaves) is a mandatory requirement that is systematically missed in assessments focused only on central fabric elements.
- Heat emitter condition and sizing is a mandatory field for heating systems — frequently omitted but critical for heat pump design decisions.
- TrustMark submission rejection reasons are dominated by five categories: missing UPRN, EPC reference error, incomplete construction data, missing moisture indicators, and incomplete occupancy data.
- Photographic evidence is a TrustMark submission requirement — a minimum pack of all elevations, boiler data plate, consumer unit, and loft access must accompany every submission.

## Self-Assessment Questions

**Q1.** A Retrofit Assessor completes a survey but does not record the occupant count or any health/vulnerability information because the occupants were not present during the survey. Under PAS 2035:2023, what must the assessor do?

A) Return to the property or arrange a remote consultation to collect the mandatory occupancy data before submitting
B) Submit the assessment with a note that the occupants were absent — this is acceptable
C) Estimate the occupant count from the property size and bedroom count
D) Omit the occupancy section — it is a recommended rather than mandatory field

**Correct answer: A**

*Occupancy data (including occupant count and health/vulnerability flags) is a mandatory Annex A field. The assessment cannot be submitted without it. The assessor must arrange to collect the data — either by revisiting, by a structured telephone or video call, or by a written questionnaire returned before submission.*

---

**Q2.** A Retrofit Assessment records the wall construction as "cavity masonry, 1965." The TrustMark data submission is rejected. Which additional mandatory field is most likely missing?

A) The wall colour — required for solar gain calculations
B) The wall height — required for U-value calculation
C) The insulation status — whether the cavity is filled or unfilled
D) The wall age band — 1965 is not a valid RdSAP age band

**Correct answer: C**

*Insulation status (filled or unfilled cavity, and insulation type if filled) is a mandatory field when wall construction is recorded. A construction type entry without insulation status is a partial entry and a submission rejection trigger.*

---

**Q3.** A property has a gas combi boiler and electric panel heaters in two bedrooms as supplementary heating. Under PAS 2035:2023 Annex A, what must the assessor record?

A) The gas combi boiler only — supplementary electric heaters are below the threshold for recording
B) The electric panel heaters only, as they are the highest-running-cost element
C) The gas combi boiler and a note that "secondary heating is present"
D) Both the gas combi boiler (primary) and the electric panel heaters (secondary heating system), including their locations

**Correct answer: D**

*Both primary and secondary heating systems must be recorded in full, including system type, fuel, location (for secondary elements), and controls. A note that secondary heating "is present" without specifics is not compliant.*

---

**Q4.** What is the UPRN and why is it a mandatory field in the TrustMark data submission?

A) The UPRN is the Unique Property Reference Number — a government-assigned identifier for every addressable property in the UK, required for data traceability and cross-referencing with other datasets
B) The UPRN is the utility rates provision number — required for fuel poverty calculations
C) The UPRN is optional — it is the Retrofit Coordinator's responsibility, not the assessor's
D) The UPRN is the utility provider reference number — required to verify gas and electricity connection

**Correct answer: A**

*The UPRN (Unique Property Reference Number) is a government-assigned unique identifier for every addressable location in Great Britain, managed by Ordnance Survey. It is mandatory for TrustMark data submission because it enables traceability, cross-referencing with EPC Register data, and data quality verification. It is the assessor's responsibility to look it up and record it accurately.*

---

**Q5.** An assessor completes a Retrofit Assessment for a property proposed for solid wall IWI. The assessment records wall construction, insulation, and moisture indicators. However, the assessor does not record the condition of the rainwater goods because they consider it irrelevant for IWI. What is the problem with this approach?

A) No problem — rainwater goods condition is only relevant for EWI, not IWI
B) The assessment is non-compliant — rainwater goods condition is a mandatory observation where any external fabric measure or moisture-sensitive measure is proposed, and IWI is a moisture-sensitive measure
C) The assessment is compliant but should note that rainwater goods were not checked
D) The assessment is compliant — rainwater goods are a recommended, not mandatory, field

**Correct answer: B**

*Rainwater goods condition is a mandatory observation under PAS 2035:2023 where moisture-sensitive measures are proposed. IWI on a solid wall is explicitly moisture-sensitive — defective rainwater goods causing penetrating damp will drive moisture into the wall fabric behind the insulation, causing failure. The assessor must record rainwater goods condition for all IWI and EWI projects.*
