

# UK Energy Policy 2026: What Assessors Need to Know

## Learning Objectives

- Identify the principal UK energy policy developments enacted or confirmed between January 2025 and March 2026 and explain their direct implications for energy assessment practice.
- Describe the key methodological changes introduced by RdSAP 10 and distinguish them from the RdSAP 9.94 framework it replaces.
- Explain the current status and practical requirements of the Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards (MEES) for domestic and non-domestic properties, including confirmed and proposed thresholds.
- Assess how the updated PAS 2035:2023 framework affects the retrofit coordinator role and the interface between energy assessors and retrofit projects.
- Apply knowledge of current policy direction to real-world lodgement scenarios, client advice, and professional development planning.

## Introduction

The period from early 2025 through the first quarter of 2026 has been one of the most consequential for energy assessors since the introduction of EPCs in 2007. The formal rollout of RdSAP 10, the ongoing evolution of MEES policy, the continued embedding of PAS 2035:2023 into publicly funded retrofit programmes, and the government's broader clean energy and housing decarbonisation agenda have collectively shifted what is expected of assessors — technically, professionally, and commercially.

This course is not a policy overview for newcomers. It is a focused update for working DEAs, NDEAs, On-Construction assessors, and retrofit professionals who need to understand, in concrete terms, what has changed, what is confirmed, what remains under consultation, and what needs to be actioned now.

The UK government's Clean Energy Superpower mission and the Warm Homes Plan, both articulated from mid-2024 onward, have set an ambitious decarbonisation trajectory. For assessors, the practical consequence is that our outputs — EPCs, recommendation reports, retrofit assessments — are increasingly load-bearing documents in regulatory compliance, property transactions, lending decisions, and grant eligibility. The tolerance for inaccuracy, inconsistency, or outdated practice is narrowing.

At the same time, the transition to a new SAP methodology is technically demanding. RdSAP 10 is not a minor revision. It alters how we handle heating systems, thermal bridging, lighting, renewables, and occupancy assumptions. Lodgement software has been updated, but the underlying logic assessors bring to site work needs to update too.

This course addresses these shifts head-on. Section 1 covers the current policy landscape and confirmed regulatory changes. Section 2 unpacks RdSAP 10 in operational terms. Section 3 addresses PAS 2035:2023 and the retrofit interface. Section 4 deals with practical application — what to do differently on Monday morning.

Every assessor reading this has a scheme obligation to maintain competence. This course helps you meet that obligation with substance, not box-ticking.

## Section 1: The Current Policy Landscape — What Is Confirmed, What Is Pending

To advise clients and produce defensible assessments, you need to distinguish between enacted regulation, confirmed policy direction, and proposals still under consultation. Conflating these categories — something we see regularly in trade press and social media — is a professional risk.

**Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards (MEES) — Non-Domestic**

The non-domestic MEES framework remains as implemented under the Energy Efficiency (Private Rented Property) (England and Wales) Regulations 2015 (as amended). Since April 2023, it has been unlawful to continue letting a non-domestic property in England and Wales with an EPC rating below E, subject to the existing exemption register provisions. The previously consulted proposal to raise the non-domestic minimum to B by 2030 has not been enacted. Assessors should advise non-domestic clients on the basis of current law — the minimum is E — while noting that further tightening remains government policy intent.

**MEES — Domestic**

The domestic private rented sector minimum stands at E, in force since April 2020 for all existing tenancies. The prior Conservative government's 2020 consultation on raising this to C by 2028 (new tenancies) and 2030 (all tenancies), with a £10,000 landlord cost cap, was never legislated. The current government has signalled its intention to raise domestic PRS standards as part of the Warm Homes Plan, but as of March 2026, no amending statutory instrument has been laid. Assessors producing EPCs for landlords should be precise: the legal requirement is E; movement toward C is policy direction, not law.

**The Warm Homes Plan and Clean Energy Mission**

The government's Warm Homes Plan, announced in the 2024 manifesto and developed through 2025, commits to upgrading five million homes over the parliamentary term through grants and low-interest loans, with a focus on insulation, heat pumps, and fuel poverty reduction. For assessors, the key operational consequence is increased demand for pre- and post-retrofit EPCs, greater integration with local authority-led schemes, and a likely expansion in the volume of PAS 2035-governed projects requiring formal retrofit assessment.

**Future Homes Standard and Future Buildings Standard**

The Future Homes Standard, confirmed for application to new dwellings from 2025 onward through amendments to Part L of the Building Regulations (England), requires new homes to produce 75–80% fewer carbon emissions than under Part L 2013. On-Construction DEAs should already be working to the updated SAP 10 methodology for new-build compliance. The parallel Future Buildings Standard for non-domestic new builds follows a similar decarbonisation trajectory.

**Devolved Administrations**

Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland maintain distinct regulatory timetables. Scottish assessors should note the continued application of the Energy Performance of Buildings (Scotland) Regulations and the separate Scottish EPC Action Plan. Welsh assessors should be aware that the Senedd has consulted on its own Optimised Retrofit Programme framework. This course focuses primarily on England and Wales, but the underlying methodological changes to SAP/RdSAP apply across all UK jurisdictions through their respective scheme requirements.

## Section 2: RdSAP 10 — What Has Actually Changed in Practice

The transition from RdSAP 9.94 to RdSAP 10 is the most significant methodological change since RdSAP 2005 was replaced by RdSAP 2009. It is not a cosmetic update. The underlying SAP 10 methodology — on which RdSAP 10 is based — revises fuel emission factors, updates default U-values, changes how heating system efficiency is calculated, and introduces updated carbon and primary energy metrics. Assessors who treat this as a software-only change and do not revisit their survey practice will produce unreliable outputs.

**Carbon Emission Factors and Fuel Pricing**

SAP 10 applies substantially revised CO₂ emission factors, most significantly for grid electricity. The emission factor for mains electricity has been reduced to reflect the ongoing decarbonisation of the UK grid since the SAP 2012 factors were set. In practice, this means that electrically heated dwellings — including those with heat pumps but also direct electric and storage heating — perform significantly better on their EPC rating under SAP 10 / RdSAP 10 than they did under the previous methodology. Conversely, gas-heated properties may see their relative position decline. This is not a change in the property; it is a change in the yardstick. Assessors must be prepared to explain this to clients, landlords, and conveyancers who compare a new EPC against a previous one.

Fuel cost assumptions have also been updated, affecting the cost-based components of the SAP rating (which underpins the EPC's A–G scale for existing dwellings). These figures are drawn from DESNZ fuel price data rather than real-time market prices, so they represent standardised assumptions, not actual bills.

**Heating Systems and Controls**

RdSAP 10 incorporates updated PCDB (Product Characteristics Database) entries for boilers, heat pumps, and other heating appliances. The treatment of smart heating controls, load compensation, and weather compensation has been refined. Assessors should ensure they are recording control types with greater specificity than may have been habitual under RdSAP 9.94. The distinction between a basic programmer/room thermostat combination and a system with load or weather compensation now carries more weight in the calculation.

Heat pump performance is handled with greater granularity. The Seasonal Coefficient of Performance (SCOP) values used in RdSAP 10 draw on updated field trial data, including data from the Electrification of Heat Demonstration Project. Where a specific product is recorded from the PCDB, its tested performance data is used; where a default is applied, the default values differ from those in SAP 2012.

**Thermal Bridging**

RdSAP 10 maintains the option of using the default y-value for thermal bridging or applying calculated psi-values from approved junction details. However, the default y-value and the way it interacts with other fabric inputs have been recalibrated. Assessors working on post-2006 properties or retrofit projects where junction details are known should be recording this with care.

**Lighting and Renewables**

The methodology now better accounts for LED lighting as the dominant technology, and the treatment of photovoltaic systems, battery storage, and solar thermal has been updated. Assessors should verify that their lodgement software correctly handles PV array data inputs under the new conventions, particularly kWp, orientation, tilt, and overshading.

**Practical Impact on EPC Ratings**

The net effect is that many properties will see a rating change when re-assessed under RdSAP 10, even if nothing physical has altered. Electrically heated properties will generally improve. Gas-heated properties may see modest declines in some cases. Assessors must understand and be able to articulate that this reflects a methodological update, not an error.

## Section 3: PAS 2035:2023 and the Retrofit Interface

PAS 2035:2023 — the third edition of the specification for the energy retrofit of domestic buildings — was published in 2023 and is now the operative version for all publicly funded retrofit work governed by the PAS 2030/2035 framework. For assessors, particularly those involved in retrofit assessment, coordination, or evaluation, the 2023 revision introduces changes that directly affect scope of work and professional responsibilities.

**Retrofit Assessment and the Assessor's Role**

PAS 2035 defines the Retrofit Assessor as a distinct role responsible for conducting a dwelling assessment, producing a condition report, and providing the data inputs that inform the Medium Term Improvement Plan and the retrofit design. Under the 2023 revision, the requirements for the dwelling assessment have been tightened, with greater emphasis on moisture risk, ventilation adequacy, and occupant factors. Assessors undertaking this role must be TrustMark-registered through an appropriate scheme and must work within a Retrofit Coordinator-led project structure.

The 2023 edition clarifies the interface between the Retrofit Assessor and the Retrofit Coordinator, particularly around the handover of assessment data and the responsibility for risk identification. Assessors should note that the Retrofit Coordinator now carries explicit responsibility for reviewing the dwelling assessment and confirming that identified risks — damp, condensation, indoor air quality, heritage constraints — are addressed in the design specification. This does not reduce the assessor's obligation to identify and record those risks accurately in the first instance.

**Risk Pathways and Moisture**

One of the most operationally significant changes in PAS 2035:2023 is the more detailed treatment of moisture risk pathways. The standard now requires that assessors and coordinators consider moisture risks at each stage — pre-installation, during works, and post-installation — and that specific risks (interstitial condensation in solid wall insulation, for example) are documented and mitigated. For assessors, this means that the dwelling assessment must go beyond a standard EPC survey in its treatment of building condition. Damp readings, ventilation observations, and occupant behaviour notes are not optional extras; they are baseline requirements.

**Ventilation**

The 2023 revision strengthens the requirement for ventilation assessment. Where retrofit measures are likely to increase airtightness — external wall insulation, internal wall insulation, window replacement, draught-proofing — PAS 2035 requires that the existing ventilation provision is assessed and, where necessary, enhanced. The assessor's role is to record the current ventilation type (natural, intermittent extract, continuous mechanical extract, MVHR) and any observable deficiencies. The Retrofit Coordinator then determines whether additional ventilation measures are required as part of the whole-house approach.

**Publicly Funded Schemes and Compliance**

All current major public retrofit funding streams in England — including the Great British Insulation Scheme (GBIS), the Home Upgrade Grant (HUG), and local authority-delivered elements of the Warm Homes Plan — require PAS 2035 compliance. Scotland's HEEPS: ABS programme and Wales's Optimised Retrofit Programme impose equivalent or analogous requirements. For assessors working within these schemes, the 2023 edition is not advisory; it is contractually and regulatorily binding.

**What This Means for Standard DEA Practice**

Even if you are not currently working as a Retrofit Assessor, the direction of travel is clear. The government's scale-up of publicly funded retrofit, combined with potential future requirements for private landlord compliance, will increasingly draw standard DEAs into the PAS 2035 orbit. Understanding the framework now — and pursuing the Retrofit Assessor qualification if appropriate — is sound professional strategy.

## Section 4: Practical Application — Adjusting Your Working Practice

Policy knowledge has no value if it does not change what you do. This section identifies specific, actionable adjustments to your survey, lodgement, and client advisory practice in light of the developments covered above.

**On Site**

Record heating controls with full specificity. Where a system uses weather compensation, load compensation, or smart thermostatic control, document the make, model, and functionality. Under RdSAP 10, these distinctions affect the output materially. If you have been defaulting to generic control descriptions, stop.

Photograph and note PV installations in full: number of panels, approximate kWp (from the inverter label or MCS certificate where available), orientation, tilt, and any overshading. RdSAP 10 handles PV with greater precision, and sloppy inputs will produce sloppy ratings.

For any property where you suspect retrofit work has been carried out — particularly insulation, windows, or heating system replacement — ask the occupant or landlord for documentation. Under both EPC and PAS 2035 frameworks, evidence-based inputs are preferred over assumptions.

**At Lodgement**

Verify that your software provider has fully implemented RdSAP 10 conventions. Check version notes and update schedules. Do not assume that a software update is complete without confirming what calculation version is being used for each lodgement.

Review your first ten RdSAP 10 lodgements with a critical eye. Compare them against what you would have expected under RdSAP 9.94. Where ratings have shifted, satisfy yourself that the change is methodologically explained, not the result of an input error.

**In Client Communication**

Prepare a brief, plain-English explanation for landlords and homeowners who query why a new EPC differs from a previous one despite no physical changes. This will be a frequent conversation through 2026 and beyond. A well-prepared assessor handles it in thirty seconds; an unprepared one creates confusion and complaints.

**In Professional Development**

If you are not yet qualified as a Retrofit Assessor under PAS 2035, evaluate whether it makes commercial and professional sense to pursue this. The volume of retrofit work flowing through publicly funded programmes is substantial, and the assessor pipeline needs to scale with it.

## Key Takeaways

- The domestic PRS MEES minimum remains at EPC Band E as of March 2026; the widely discussed move to C is policy intent, not enacted law. Advise clients accordingly and avoid stating proposed changes as settled requirements.
- RdSAP 10 is a substantive methodological change, not a minor update. Revised grid electricity emission factors mean that electrically heated properties will generally see improved ratings and gas-heated properties may see relative declines, independent of any physical change.
- Heating control specification matters more under RdSAP 10. Record control types — particularly weather compensation, load compensation, and smart TRVs — with precision and evidence.
- PAS 2035:2023 tightens the dwelling assessment requirements, especially around moisture risk, ventilation, and the handover between Retrofit Assessor and Retrofit Coordinator. If you are working within funded retrofit schemes, ensure your practice meets the current edition, not the 2019 original.
- EPC ratings will shift across the stock as RdSAP 10 is applied. Be prepared to explain methodological recalibration to clients, conveyancers, lenders, and letting agents clearly and accurately.
- The Future Homes Standard is now operative for new dwellings. On-Construction assessors must be working to SAP 10 for Part L compliance and should verify their software and workflow against the current Approved Documents.
- Professional development toward Retrofit Assessor or Retrofit Coordinator qualification is a strategically sound investment given the trajectory of publicly funded and regulatory-driven retrofit demand.

## Self-Assessment Questions

**Question 1**
Under the current MEES regulations for domestic private rented properties in England and Wales (as of March 2026), what is the minimum EPC rating required?

A) Band D
B) Band C
C) Band E
D) Band F

**Correct answer: C**
*The domestic PRS minimum of EPC Band E has been in force for all tenancies since April 2020. The proposed increase to Band C has not been enacted in legislation.*

---

**Question 2**
What is the primary reason that an electrically heated dwelling may receive a better EPC rating under RdSAP 10 compared to RdSAP 9.94, even if no physical changes have been made to the property?

A) RdSAP 10 excludes electricity from the calculation
B) RdSAP 10 uses a reduced CO₂ emission factor for grid electricity, reflecting grid decarbonisation
C) RdSAP 10 applies a higher efficiency default to all electric heating systems
D) RdSAP 10 ignores heating fuel type and rates only on fabric performance

**Correct answer: B**
*SAP 10 updates the CO₂ emission factor for mains electricity to reflect the significant decarbonisation of the UK grid since the SAP 2012 factors were established. This improves the environmental rating of electrically heated dwellings.*

---

**Question 3**
Under PAS 2035:2023, which role carries explicit responsibility for reviewing the dwelling assessment and confirming that identified risks are addressed in the retrofit design specification?

A) The Retrofit Assessor
B) The Retrofit Installer
C) The Retrofit Coordinator
D) The Energy Assessor (DEA)

**Correct answer: C**
*PAS 2035:2023 clarifies that the Retrofit Coordinator reviews the dwelling assessment and ensures that identified risks — including moisture, ventilation, and heritage constraints — are incorporated into the design. The Assessor's obligation is to identify and record the risks accurately.*

---

**Question 4**
The Future Homes Standard, as applied to new dwellings in England, requires what approximate level of CO₂ emission reduction compared to Part L 2013?

A) 30–35%
B) 50–55%
C) 75–80%
D) 100% (net zero operational carbon)

**Correct answer: C**
*The Future Homes Standard requires new homes to produce 75–80% fewer CO₂ emissions than under the Part L 2013 baseline, primarily through improved fabric performance and low-carbon heating.*

---

**Question 5**
When producing an EPC under RdSAP 10 for a dwelling with a photovoltaic installation, which of the following data points is NOT a required input for the PV calculation?

A) Peak power output (kWp) of the array
B) Orientation and tilt of the panels
C) The retail electricity tariff paid by the occupant
D) Overshading of the array

**Correct answer: C**
*RdSAP 10 uses standardised fuel price assumptions from DESNZ data, not actual occupant tariffs. The PV calculation requires kWp, orientation, tilt, and overshading as inputs.*
