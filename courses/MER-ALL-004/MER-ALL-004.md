

# Fire Safety in Retrofit: What Every Assessor Must Know
**Course ID:** MER-ALL-004 | **CPD Hours:** 1.0 | **Published:** March 2026

---

## Learning Objectives

- Identify the principal fire safety risks introduced by common domestic retrofit measures, including external wall insulation (EWI), internal wall insulation (IWI), cavity wall insulation, and roof/loft interventions.
- Explain the regulatory framework governing fire safety in retrofit, including the Building Regulations 2010 Approved Document B, the Building Safety Act 2022, and the requirements embedded within PAS 2035:2023.
- Recognise how the assessor's role within the PAS 2035 retrofit process creates specific obligations to identify, flag, and escalate fire safety risks to the Retrofit Coordinator.
- Apply a structured risk-identification approach when surveying dwellings, with particular attention to compartmentation, cavity barriers, means of escape, and reaction-to-fire classification of materials.
- Determine when a retrofit measure recommendation requires referral for specialist fire risk assessment, and document that referral appropriately within assessment records and medium-term improvement plans.

## Introduction

Fire safety in domestic retrofit is not a peripheral concern. It is now a central regulatory and professional obligation, and the assessor who treats it as somebody else's problem is operating outside current standards.

The post-Grenfell regulatory landscape has reshaped how we think about materials, cavities, and compartmentation in existing buildings. The Building Safety Act 2022 extended accountability across the entire construction and refurbishment supply chain. PAS 2035:2023 explicitly requires that retrofit projects do not degrade the fire safety performance of the dwelling. That requirement flows directly through the assessment stage: if you are the Retrofit Assessor conducting the dwelling survey, you are the first professional pair of eyes on the building. What you record — and what you fail to record — has consequences.

This is not theoretical. Post-installation fires linked to incorrectly specified cavity wall insulation, EWI systems installed without adequate cavity barriers at party wall junctions, and combustible insulation materials placed in proximity to heat sources have all occurred in the UK housing stock. In several cases, the original assessment failed to note conditions that should have triggered a referral or a constraint on the recommended measures.

The expansion of publicly funded retrofit programmes — the Great British Insulation Scheme, the Warm Homes Plan, ECO4 and its successors — has accelerated the volume of installations in building typologies that carry elevated fire risk: system-built housing, timber-framed dwellings, properties with compromised or absent cavity barriers, and buildings where previous alterations have breached compartmentation. Assessors working across these programmes encounter these archetypes routinely.

This course does not attempt to make you a fire engineer. It aims to equip you with the working knowledge to identify fire safety risks during assessment, understand the material and regulatory constraints that apply, and document and escalate appropriately. In a PAS 2035 project, the Retrofit Coordinator holds design responsibility, but the Coordinator's risk decisions are only as good as the data you provide. Your survey is the foundation.

The content that follows is structured around risk identification, regulatory requirements, material specifications, and practical application to your assessment workflow.

## Section 1: The Retrofit Fire Risk Landscape — What Goes Wrong and Why

The core fire safety risks in domestic retrofit cluster around four mechanisms: the introduction of combustible materials, the creation or bridging of cavities, the breach of existing compartmentation, and the obstruction of means of escape. Each maps directly to common retrofit measures.

**External Wall Insulation (EWI)** systems present the most widely discussed risk, particularly on buildings above 11 metres in height where Approved Document B restricts the use of combustible materials in external walls under Regulation 7(2) of the Building Regulations (as amended 2018). However, assessors working on houses and low-rise blocks must not assume the risk is negligible. EWI systems use a combination of insulation board, adhesive, mechanical fixings, mesh reinforcement, and render. The European classification system (BS EN 13501-1) grades reaction to fire from A1 (non-combustible) to F (no performance determined). Expanded polystyrene (EPS), the most commonly used EWI insulation in domestic retrofit, typically achieves Class E without flame retardant treatment, though systems using EPS with flame retardant additives may achieve Class B or C as part of a tested system. The critical point for assessors: fire performance is a system property, not a product property. An EPS board tested in isolation performs differently from an EPS board within a complete system with mineral wool fire barriers at specified intervals.

**Cavity Wall Insulation (CWI)** introduces material into a cavity that was originally designed, in part, as a fire barrier. In masonry cavity construction, the cavity itself contributes to compartmentation between dwellings in semi-detached and terraced housing. The installation of CWI can bridge that cavity, and if cavity barriers at party wall junctions are absent, damaged, or bypassed, fire and smoke can propagate laterally between dwellings. BRE research has documented cases where blown mineral fibre or bead insulation filled the cavity continuously across party wall boundaries. For system-built properties with non-traditional cavity configurations, the risk is amplified.

**Internal Wall Insulation (IWI)** reduces room dimensions and frequently necessitates moving electrical sockets, repositioning heat emitters, and altering window reveals. Each alteration can breach existing compartmentation — particularly at party walls and at floor-to-wall junctions. Additionally, some IWI products incorporate vapour control layers or insulation boards with facings that have a higher surface spread of flame classification than the original wall finish.

**Loft and Roof Insulation** measures, while generally lower risk, can introduce combustible material in proximity to electrical cables, recessed downlighters, and flue penetrations. Assessors should note the presence of non-fire-rated downlighters and any flue pipes that pass through the insulated zone, both of which require specific detailing or exclusion zones.

The assessor's role at survey stage is not to resolve these risks but to create a complete record that enables the Retrofit Coordinator to address them. That means documenting construction type, cavity configuration, party wall condition, existing fire-stopping, means of escape routes, and the presence of any features — such as timber-framed party walls, plastic cavity trays, or combustible sheathing boards — that impose constraints on measure selection.

## Section 2: Regulatory Framework — Approved Document B, Building Safety Act, and PAS 2035:2023

Assessors must understand where fire safety obligations originate and how they interact across the regulatory and standards landscape. The system is layered, and retrofit sits at the intersection of building control, occupational safety, and the PAS 2035 quality framework.

**Approved Document B (Fire Safety), Volume 1 — Dwellings** provides statutory guidance on compliance with the fire safety requirements of the Building Regulations 2010 (as amended). It addresses means of warning and escape (B1), internal fire spread — linings (B2), internal fire spread — structure (B3), external fire spread (B4), and access and facilities for the fire service (B5). Retrofit works that constitute a "material alteration" or a "change of use" under Regulation 3 or Regulation 5 trigger compliance requirements. Critically, Regulation 3(2) states that a material alteration must not cause the building to fail to comply with a relevant requirement where it previously did comply, or to become more unsatisfactory in relation to a relevant requirement where it already failed to comply. This "no worsening" principle is directly analogous to the PAS 2035 requirement not to degrade performance.

The 2018 amendment to Regulation 7 (Materials and workmanship) introduced the ban on combustible materials in the external walls of buildings with a storey at least 18 metres above ground level, later reduced to 11 metres by the 2020 amendment. While most domestic retrofit work falls below this threshold, assessors surveying low-rise blocks of flats should verify storey height relative to these thresholds.

**The Building Safety Act 2022** established the Building Safety Regulator within the Health and Safety Executive and introduced a new accountability framework. While the higher-risk building regime (Part 4 of the Act) targets buildings at least 18 metres or 7 storeys, the Act's broader provisions — including the extension of limitation periods for claims under the Defective Premises Act 1972 to 30 years for retrospective claims — have implications across the housing stock. Any assessor involved in retrofit that subsequently contributes to a fire safety deficiency is operating in a more litigious environment than existed pre-2022.

**PAS 2035:2023** addresses fire safety explicitly. Clause 6 requires the Retrofit Coordinator to ensure that retrofit measures do not reduce fire safety. The dwelling assessment (Clause 7) must identify risks including fire safety hazards. The Medium Term Improvement Plan and the retrofit design must account for fire risk, and the installation specification must reference appropriate fire-stopping and cavity barrier requirements. The standard does not delegate fire risk assessment to the assessor, but it does require the assessor to capture the dwelling data on which fire risk decisions depend. An assessment that omits construction type at party walls, fails to record the presence of combustible materials in the external wall build-up, or does not note the absence of a fire detection system is an incomplete assessment under PAS 2035.

**RdSAP 10** introduces improved data capture for wall construction, insulation type, and building services. Assessors should be aware that while the energy model itself does not calculate fire risk, the data fields — particularly those relating to wall type, insulation, and construction date — provide information relevant to fire safety evaluation by the Retrofit Coordinator. Accurate recording in RdSAP supports downstream fire safety decisions.

For social housing, the Decent Homes Standard review and the Social Housing (Regulation) Act 2023 further reinforce the expectation that retrofit works do not compromise fire safety. Registered providers are subject to proactive inspection by the Regulator of Social Housing, and assessors working on social stock should expect their survey outputs to be scrutinised in this context.

## Section 3: Material Classifications, Cavity Barriers, and Compartmentation — The Technical Detail

Fire safety in retrofit ultimately comes down to materials, junctions, and compartments. Assessors need working familiarity with the classification systems, the cavity barrier requirements, and the compartmentation principles that govern what can be installed and where.

**Reaction to Fire Classification (BS EN 13501-1)** replaced the older British Standard "Class 0 / Class 1" surface spread of flame system, though Approved Document B still references both frameworks in transitional provisions. The Euroclass system runs from A1 (non-combustible, e.g., stone wool, glass) through A2 (limited combustibility, e.g., some mineral wool products with binders), B (very limited contribution to fire, e.g., fire-retardant treated timber, some phenolic foams), C, D (e.g., some wood-based products), E (e.g., untreated EPS), to F (no performance determined). The suffix "s" denotes smoke production (s1, s2, s3) and "d" denotes flaming droplets (d0, d1, d2). When recording existing insulation or noting proposed insulation types in your assessment, the Euroclass rating is the specification language the Coordinator and installer will work with.

For external wall systems, BRE 135 provides the test methodology for fire performance of external cladding systems, and BS 8414 is the large-scale test standard. Systems that pass BS 8414 and meet the BR 135 criteria can be used on buildings above 11 metres even if individual components are not Class A1/A2. Assessors surveying dwellings with existing EWI should note the system type and, where possible, the insulation material, as this informs risk assessment for any further works.

**Cavity Barriers** are the primary mechanism for limiting fire spread within concealed cavities. Approved Document B, Section 9, requires cavity barriers at specified locations including: the junction between an external cavity wall and a compartment wall (party wall); around openings in a cavity wall (windows, doors); at the top of a cavity wall where it meets the roof; and at maximum intervals within extensive cavities. For timber-framed buildings, cavity barrier requirements are more onerous due to the combustibility of the structural frame.

In retrofit, cavity barriers are compromised in two common scenarios. First, CWI installation that fills the cavity continuously without interruption at party wall junctions, effectively creating a fire pathway between dwellings. Industry guidance — including CIGA (Cavity Insulation Guarantee Agency) technical requirements and BRE guidance — requires that the cavity at the party wall junction is checked and, where necessary, fire-stopped before or during insulation installation. The assessor's role is to identify the party wall configuration and flag it. Second, EWI installation that creates a new cavity between the insulation board and the existing wall finish (for example, where mechanical fixings hold the board off the wall surface) without cavity barriers at compartment wall lines and around openings. Again, documentation of the existing wall condition and party wall locations is essential.

**Compartmentation** in domestic buildings primarily concerns the separation between dwellings in flats and between attached houses. A compartment wall must provide a specified period of fire resistance (typically 60 minutes in houses, 30 or 60 minutes in flats depending on building height, as per Table 6 in Approved Document B Volume 1). Retrofit measures that penetrate, bridge, or bypass compartment walls compromise this fire resistance. IWI applied to a party wall, if not correctly detailed, can reduce the effective fire resistance of the compartment wall. Services penetrations made during retrofit — for mechanical ventilation, for example — must be fire-stopped to maintain compartmentation.

Assessors should note the condition of compartment walls during survey, including any evidence of previous breaches, gaps at junctions with floors or ceilings, and the presence or absence of fire-stopping around existing service penetrations. This data feeds directly into the Coordinator's risk assessment.

## Section 4: Practical Application — What to Record, When to Refer, How to Document

Your assessment workflow should integrate fire safety identification as a standard element, not an afterthought. The following framework maps fire safety observations onto your existing survey process.

**At the External Survey Stage**, record: the construction type and material of external walls (masonry, timber frame, system build, steel frame); the presence and type of any existing EWI or cladding; the location and configuration of party walls as visible from the exterior; evidence of cavity trays, weep holes, and their material (plastic cavity trays are combustible); the proximity of the building to boundaries and neighbouring structures; and the height of the building in storeys. Photograph party wall junctions at eaves level and any visible fire-stopping.

**At the Internal Survey Stage**, record: wall construction at party walls (tap-test, note dry-lining, identify any cavity); the condition of fire-stopping around service penetrations through party walls, floors, and ceilings; the presence and type of fire detection (smoke alarms, heat detectors — note whether mains-wired or battery, and their locations relative to current BS 5839-6 recommendations); the presence of recessed downlighters in ceilings below loft spaces; the type and location of heating appliances, including flue routes; and the means of escape from each storey (window dimensions and accessibility for rooms accessed via a single staircase in three-storey dwellings).

**Referral Triggers** — the following conditions should prompt a clear note in your assessment for the Retrofit Coordinator, flagging the need for specialist input or constraining the recommended measures:

- Timber-framed party walls or external walls where CWI or EWI is under consideration.
- System-built construction types listed in BRE's non-traditional housing documentation, particularly those with known fire safety concerns (e.g., Wimpey No-Fines, Airey, BISF).
- Absent, damaged, or uncertain cavity barriers at party wall junctions.
- Properties in purpose-built or converted flats where compartmentation integrity is uncertain.
- Any dwelling above 11 metres where external wall works are proposed.

**Documentation** should be explicit. Avoid vague language. Write "Party wall at first floor: dry-lined with plasterboard on timber battens, no evidence of fire-stopping at junction with ceiling — refer to Retrofit Coordinator for fire risk assessment prior to IWI specification." This is the standard your assessment should meet. If it is not in your report, it did not happen.

## Key Takeaways

- Fire safety is an explicit requirement of PAS 2035:2023 — retrofit measures must not degrade the fire safety performance of the dwelling, and the assessor's survey is the primary source of data for this evaluation.
- The "no worsening" principle in Building Regulation 3(2) has direct application to retrofit: if the building currently complies with a fire safety requirement, your recommended measures must not cause it to fail.
- Record construction type, party wall configuration, cavity barrier presence, compartmentation condition, fire detection provision, and means of escape as standard elements of every dwelling assessment.
- Material fire performance is a system property — individual product Euroclass ratings do not determine the performance of an assembled EWI or IWI system.
- Cavity wall insulation in dwellings with shared party walls requires confirmation that the cavity is interrupted at the party wall junction; flag this explicitly if it cannot be confirmed at survey.
- Timber-framed construction, system-built housing, and converted flats are elevated-risk archetypes — always flag these for Retrofit Coordinator review before measure specification proceeds.
- Document your fire safety observations in clear, specific language and ensure referral triggers are recorded as explicit actions, not buried in general notes.

## Self-Assessment Questions

**Question 1.** Under Regulation 3(2) of the Building Regulations 2010, a material alteration must not:

A) Change the external appearance of the building without planning consent
B) Cause the building to fail to comply with a relevant requirement where it previously complied, or become more unsatisfactory where it already failed
C) Increase the energy consumption of the building above its current baseline
D) Require a new EPC to be lodged on the register

**Correct answer: B.**
Regulation 3(2) establishes the "no worsening" principle. This applies directly to fire safety requirements and is the regulatory basis for ensuring retrofit does not degrade fire performance.

---

**Question 2.** Under the Euroclass reaction to fire classification system (BS EN 13501-1), which class represents the lowest fire performance for which a classification has been determined?

A) Class F
B) Class E
C) Class D
D) Class A2

**Correct answer: B.**
Class E is the lowest classified performance. Class F means "no performance determined" — the product has not been tested or has failed to achieve Class E. Untreated expanded polystyrene (EPS) typically achieves Class E.

---

**Question 3.** Which of the following conditions should an assessor flag as a referral trigger for specialist fire risk input before CWI is specified?

A) The property has a south-facing rear elevation
B) The property is a mid-terrace house with party walls and no evidence of cavity barriers at party wall junctions
C) The property was built after 2006 and already has partial cavity fill
D) The property has uPVC fascia boards

**Correct answer: B.**
Absent or uncertain cavity barriers at party wall junctions in terraced or semi-detached properties create a risk of fire spread between dwellings via the insulated cavity. This must be flagged for the Retrofit Coordinator.

---

**Question 4.** PAS 2035:2023 assigns responsibility for ensuring that retrofit measures do not reduce fire safety to which role?

A) The Retrofit Assessor
B) The Retrofit Installer
C) The Retrofit Coordinator
D) The building control body

**Correct answer: C.**
The Retrofit Coordinator holds design and risk management responsibility under PAS 2035. However, the Coordinator's ability to discharge this duty depends on the quality and completeness of the assessor's dwelling survey data.

---

**Question 5.** At what building height threshold did the 2020 amendment to Building Regulation 7 restrict the use of combustible materials in external walls?

A) 18 metres
B) 15 metres
C) 11 metres
D) 7 metres

**Correct answer: C.**
The original 2018 ban applied at 18 metres. The 2020 amendment lowered the threshold to 11 metres. Assessors surveying low-rise blocks of flats should verify height relative to this threshold when external wall measures are under consideration.
