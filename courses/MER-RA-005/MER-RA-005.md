

# Solid Wall Insulation: IWI vs EWI — Risks, Evidence and Assessment
**Course ID:** MER-RA-005 | **CPD Hours:** 1.0 | **Published:** March 2026
*© Meridian CPD 2026. All rights reserved.*

---

## Learning Objectives

- Apply the risk classification framework for solid wall insulation (IWI and EWI) under PAS 2035:2023 and determine the appropriate risk pathway for a given property.
- Identify the key moisture, thermal bridging, and structural risk factors for both IWI and EWI installations and record them accurately in the Retrofit Assessment.
- Recognise the heritage and planning constraints that affect EWI viability and understand the assessor's duty to identify and record these constraints.
- Record the mandatory pre-installation condition requirements for IWI and EWI and explain why unresolved defects are a barrier to measure installation.
- Distinguish the Retrofit Assessor's data collection role from the Retrofit Designer's specification role in solid wall insulation projects.

---

## Introduction

Solid wall insulation — whether internal or external — is consistently among the highest-impact and highest-risk retrofit measures in UK housing. It is also consistently among the most poorly assessed. The consequence of inadequate assessment is not an incorrect EPC rating; it is an insulation failure that causes structural damage, mould growth, occupant ill-health, and the need for costly remediation — sometimes within a single winter.

PAS 2035:2023 assigns all solid wall insulation projects a risk level that requires at least Path B oversight, and in many cases Path C with specialist investigation. This is not bureaucracy for its own sake — it reflects the genuine complexity and consequence of getting solid wall insulation wrong. The assessor who understands why these projects are high-risk, and who records the data that reflects that risk accurately, enables the design and installation process to manage it. The assessor who treats solid wall insulation as a routine survey item, recording only the construction type and moving on, is the weak link in a chain that can fail catastrophically.

This course covers the risk differentiation between IWI and EWI, the assessment data requirements for each, heritage and planning constraints, and pre-installation conditions.

## Section 1: IWI — Internal Wall Insulation Risk Factors and Assessment Data

Internal wall insulation involves applying insulation to the internal face of an external wall, reducing the room depth and bringing the insulated surface into the internal environment. The key risk is interstitial condensation: moisture-laden internal air diffuses through the insulation into the original wall, where it contacts the cold masonry surface and condenses. Over time, this saturates the masonry, degrades the insulation, and can cause structural damage.

**Moisture risk factors specific to IWI.** The interstitial condensation risk for IWI is a function of: the vapour resistance of the insulation product (rigid PIR, phenolic foam, and some composite boards have higher vapour resistance than mineral wool); the vapour resistance of any finishes applied to the insulation; the vapour pressure gradient (driven by occupant behaviour, ventilation, and internal conditions); and the drying potential of the wall (how easily moisture can dry to the exterior, which depends on wall permeability and exposure). In high-occupancy dwellings with poor ventilation, or in Very Severe exposure zones where external drying is limited, the risk is elevated.

**Mandatory assessment data for IWI projects:**
- Wall construction (solid masonry type, stone type if applicable)
- Wall condition: pointing, render condition, crack patterns, DPC integrity
- Evidence of existing moisture (rising, penetrating, or condensation — per MER-RA-002)
- Exposure zone
- Internal room depths and ceiling heights (to assess spatial impact of IWI on habitable space)
- Presence and condition of any existing internal finishes that would need to be removed (cornicing, dado rails, fitted furniture fixed to external walls, skirtings)
- Electrical and plumbing services on external walls (must be accommodated within the insulation thickness or relocated — a design decision, but a data input from the assessment)
- Window reveal depth and junction detail at reveals (reveals will be further recessed after IWI — the visual and thermal impact must be noted)

**What IWI cannot proceed without.** IWI is a moisture-sensitive measure. PAS 2035:2023 is unambiguous that active moisture problems must be resolved before IWI installation. An IWI system installed over active rising damp or active penetrating damp will trap moisture, accelerate structural degradation, and fail. The assessor must flag any active moisture as a pre-condition — resolution of the moisture issue must be confirmed by specialist investigation and remediation before IWI proceeds.

## Section 2: EWI — External Wall Insulation Risk Factors and Assessment Data

External wall insulation involves applying insulation and an external finish (typically render, cladding, or brick slip) to the external face of the wall. The key structural risk is that EWI loads onto the wall and its anchoring — wall fixings must be adequate for the additional load, and in some masonry types (soft lime mortar, friable stone), mechanical fixing is problematic. The key moisture risk is at the junctions: where EWI terminates (at the DPC, at the roof eaves, at window reveals), the junction detailing must prevent moisture ingress.

**Mandatory assessment data for EWI projects:**
- Wall construction and condition (as for IWI)
- External finish type and condition — render, faced brick, stone, tile hanging. EWI is typically applied over a prepared substrate; defective existing render may need to be removed. The assessor records the current finish and its condition.
- Window and door recess depths — windows will be further recessed into the reveals after EWI. Where reveals are already at minimum depth, the design may need to incorporate EWI-compatible window replacement as part of the project.
- Soffit and eaves detail — EWI must terminate correctly at the eaves. Where the existing soffit depth is insufficient to accommodate the insulation thickness without a visible band of insulation below the soffit line, the design must account for this. The assessor records the existing eaves configuration.
- DPC position — EWI must terminate at or above DPC level. In properties where the DPC is close to ground level or where pathways have been raised over the DPC, the EWI termination detail is critical.
- Drainage outlets, meter boxes, and external services — all penetrations through the EWI must be detailed and sealed. The assessor records their location and type.
- Condition of mortar joints and fixings — if the existing masonry cannot support mechanical fixings, EWI is not viable without remediation.

## Section 3: Heritage, Planning, and Conservation Constraints

EWI changes the external appearance of a building. In many cases, this is a planning matter, and in some cases it is a legal constraint that makes EWI impossible without listed building consent or planning permission that may not be granted.

**Listed buildings.** Any property on the statutory list (Grade I, II*, or II) requires listed building consent for any work that affects the character of the building. EWI applied to a listed building without consent is a criminal offence. The assessor must check the listed building status for every property assessed for EWI. This is a free check via Historic England's National Heritage List for England (NHLE) or the equivalent registers in Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland.

**Conservation areas.** Properties within a designated conservation area may require planning permission for changes to the external appearance. Local authority planning policies vary — some conservation areas have Article 4 directions removing permitted development rights for external changes, while others do not. The assessor should record whether the property is in a conservation area (checkable via the local planning authority's online map or the planning portal) and note that planning advice should be sought before EWI is specified.

**Traditional construction.** Properties of traditional construction (pre-1919 solid masonry, stone, clay lump, or cob) present specific constraints. EWI on traditional construction must use vapour-open materials to maintain the breathability of the fabric — the original wall relies on its ability to absorb and release moisture rather than to resist it. Vapour-impermeable EWI systems on traditional construction can cause rapid and severe moisture accumulation within the wall fabric. The assessor should note traditional construction type and flag the breathability requirement as a design constraint.

## Section 4: Pre-Installation Conditions and Path Assignment

**Path assignment for solid wall insulation.** PAS 2035:2023 assigns all solid wall insulation projects to at least Path B. Path C is required where:
- Active moisture problems are identified
- The property is in Very Severe exposure zone and solid wall insulation is proposed
- Traditional or historic construction is involved
- The assessor has identified significant uncertainty about the wall's suitability that requires specialist investigation to resolve

**Pre-installation conditions (mandatory assessment flag).** For every solid wall insulation project, the assessment must identify and flag any conditions that must be resolved before installation. The key pre-installation conditions are:
- Resolution of active moisture (rising, penetrating, condensation)
- Repair of failed pointing, cracked render, or structurally defective masonry
- Clearance of planning and heritage constraints (listed building consent, conservation area approval)
- Specialist investigation where wall construction is uncertain or complex (e.g., unusual stone type, unidentified system build)

These pre-conditions are not recommendations from the assessor — they are data flags that inform the Retrofit Coordinator's project plan. The Coordinator determines whether and how to address them; the assessor identifies that they exist.

**The assessor's limiting principle.** In solid wall insulation projects more than almost any other, the assessor must resist the temptation to make design recommendations. Record what is present. Flag what needs investigation. Let the design process make the decisions. An assessor who writes "IWI suitable subject to DPC treatment" has made a design determination they are not qualified to make and have created a liability for themselves.

## Key Takeaways

- All solid wall insulation projects require at least Path B under PAS 2035:2023; Path C applies where active moisture, Very Severe exposure, or traditional construction is involved.
- IWI carries interstitial condensation risk that is a function of vapour resistance, occupant conditions, and wall permeability — the assessor records the data that drives this risk assessment, not the risk assessment itself.
- EWI changes the external appearance of the building — listed building status and conservation area location are mandatory checks for any EWI project.
- Traditional construction (pre-1919 solid masonry, stone, cob) requires vapour-open insulation systems — the construction type must be recorded explicitly to flag this design constraint.
- Pre-installation conditions (active moisture, masonry defects, planning consents) must be flagged in the assessment as barriers to installation — they are data flags, not assessor recommendations.
- Window and door reveal depth, eaves detail, and services penetrations are mandatory EWI data fields that are systematically omitted in assessments not specifically tailored to EWI projects.

## Self-Assessment Questions

**Q1.** A Retrofit Assessor surveys a 1905 stone-built terrace for EWI. The stone is soft limestone with lime mortar pointing. What critical design constraint must the assessor flag in the assessment?

A) The cost of EWI on stone is typically prohibitive — the assessor should recommend a different measure
B) The assessor should check whether the wall is structurally sound enough for mechanical fixings
C) The traditional construction requires vapour-open insulation systems to maintain fabric breathability — the design must specify materials compatible with the breathable wall
D) Stone walls cannot receive EWI under any circumstances — the assessment should note this as a barrier

**Correct answer: C**

*Soft limestone with lime mortar is traditional construction that relies on vapour-open behaviour to manage moisture. EWI systems must use vapour-open materials (typically lime-based renders and mineral fibre or wood fibre insulation boards) to maintain breathability. Vapour-impermeable EWI on traditional construction causes moisture accumulation and structural damage. The assessor flags the construction type; the designer selects the appropriate system.*

**Q2.** During a Retrofit Assessment for IWI, the assessor observes active penetrating damp on the north gable wall, traceable to a defective chimney flashing. What is the correct assessment action?

A) Record the active penetrating damp, identify the source (chimney flashing), flag it as a mandatory pre-condition that must be resolved before IWI can proceed on any elevation
B) Classify the project as Path A — the defect is minor and easily resolved
C) Note the defect and advise the client to repair the flashing before the survey report is finalised
D) Record the IWI as suitable on the other elevations and recommend IWI proceed on the dry elevations first

**Correct answer: A**

*Active penetrating damp is a pre-installation pre-condition under PAS 2035:2023 — IWI cannot proceed until the moisture source is resolved and the wall has dried. The assessor records the evidence and flags it in the assessment data as a pre-condition. The project is also likely Path C given active moisture in a solid-wall property proposed for a moisture-sensitive measure.*

---

**Q3.** An assessor is surveying a 1890s semi-detached property in a conservation area for EWI. What check must the assessor make, and why?

A) Check whether the property is within 10 metres of a listed building
B) No specific check is required — conservation areas do not restrict external works to dwellings
C) Check whether the property is listed — conservation area properties have the same status as listed buildings
D) Check whether the conservation area has Article 4 directions removing permitted development rights for changes to external appearance, and record this as a planning constraint requiring resolution before EWI can proceed

**Correct answer: D**

*Conservation area designation does not automatically prevent EWI, but many conservation areas have Article 4 directions removing permitted development rights for external changes. The assessor must record the conservation area status and flag that planning advice (and potentially planning permission) is required. This is a data flag, not a design determination.*

---

**Q4.** A 1975 brick-built semi-detached property is assessed for IWI. The assessor notes that the property has alcove shelving and a fitted wardrobe fixed to the main external rear wall. What must be recorded in the assessment?

A) The furniture should be recorded only if it is at least 50% of the wall area
B) The presence of built-in furniture and services fixed to the external wall, noting that these will need to be removed or accommodated before IWI can be installed — this is a pre-installation condition and a cost factor for the client and designer
C) Nothing — fitted furniture is not relevant to IWI assessment
D) The assessor should recommend that the client removes the furniture before the design stage

**Correct answer: B**

*Fixed furniture and services on external walls are mandatory data items for IWI assessment. They represent a pre-installation condition (furniture must be removed) and a design challenge (services must be re-routed or accommodated within the insulation thickness). The assessor records their presence; the Retrofit Coordinator and client address the pre-conditions.*

---

**Q5.** What is the key difference between a Retrofit Assessor's assessment of solid wall insulation suitability and a Retrofit Designer's determination?

A) The assessor determines suitability; the designer confirms it
B) There is no difference — both roles assess suitability
C) The assessor determines suitability for IWI; the designer determines suitability for EWI
D) The assessor records factual data about the wall and its condition; the designer uses that data to determine whether insulation is appropriate, select the product and system, and specify any ancillary measures

**Correct answer: D**

*The assessor's role is data collection — recording wall construction, condition, moisture, exposure, planning constraints, and pre-installation conditions. The determination of suitability, product selection, and system specification is the designer's role. This boundary is explicit in PAS 2035:2023 and essential to maintain — assessors who make suitability determinations exceed their scope and create liability risk.*
